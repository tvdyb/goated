# Phase 04 — Discretionary Strategies: How Expert Traders Work the Soybean Complex

## Abstract

Discretionary soybean trading is not a single craft; it is a family of decision frameworks held together by the structure of the CBOT complex (ZS/ZM/ZL), the USDA reporting calendar, and the physical-delivery convergence mechanism. Within that scaffolding, named practitioners have built durable playbooks: spread traders express relative-value views through the board crush, oilshare, and July/November calendar; report traders pre-position into WASDE, Export Sales, and Grain Stocks and then fade or follow depending on surprise direction; weather traders size positions into ENSO-biased windows; basis traders work country-versus-export differentials against river logistics; and positioning traders read the CFTC Disaggregated COT for crowded-trade inflections. This survey catalogs those playbooks against a named-source requirement — Jack Schwager's *Market Wizards* volumes, Peter Brandt's Factor letter, Richard Dennis and the Turtles, Linda Raschke's *Street Smarts*, the Hightower Report, Pro Farmer's Crop Tour, Allendale, StoneX, AgResource, and farmdoc — and closes with documented blowup case studies from 2012, 2018, and 2022 that discipline every framework discussed above.

## 1. Spread trading: the first-order relative-value game

### 1.1 The crush spread — board versus GPM

The soybean complex is defined by the physical conversion ratio — one 60-pound bushel yields roughly 11 lb of oil and 44 lb of meal ([CME, "Understanding Soybean Crush"](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/courses/introduction-to-agriculture/grains-oilseeds/understanding-soybean-crush)). On the board, practitioners trade the exchange-listed 10:11:9 package (10 ZM + 9 ZL − 11 ZS) or the simpler long-beans / short-products "reverse crush" when they think processor margins will compress ([CME, "Soybean Crush Reference Guide"](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/files/soybean-crush-reference-guide.pdf)).

The cleanest practitioner framing comes from the distinction between the *board crush* and the *gross processing margin* (GPM) at the plant. The board is a hedging instrument; GPM is operating reality ([StoneX, "What is soybean crush in trading?"](https://www.stonex.com/en/financial-glossary/soybean-crush/)). When physical GPM runs at $2+/bu — which StoneX, AgResource, and the Hightower Report all flagged during the 2024–2026 renewable-diesel build-out — crushers sell meal and oil forward and lock in margin by buying the board crush; when board crush is cheap relative to GPM, speculators can put on a long-crush / short-reverse-crush position and effectively be paid for taking the processor's risk ([Agweb WASDE commentary on crush vs. exports](https://www.agweb.com/markets/market-analysis/soybeans-fade-china-buys-corn-wheat-fail-pre-wasde-hitting-resistance); [FarmProgress, "High crush margins drive rapid expansion"](https://www.farmprogress.com/soybean/high-crush-margins-drive-rapid-expansion)).

Academic work complements the practitioner framing. Rechner and Poitras (1993), surveying intraday spread reversal in the soy complex across 1978–1991, report that a naïve crush mean-reversion trade is a loser after 1.5¢/bu transaction costs unless the trader adds a filter; with a 3¢ entry filter, expected profit per trade rises from −0.35¢/bu to +1.74¢/bu ([Rechner & Poitras, "Putting on the Crush"](https://www.sfu.ca/~poitras/soy.pdf); [Simon (2010), "Soybean Futures Crush Spread Arbitrage"](https://www.mdpi.com/1911-8074/3/1/63)). The reverse spread exhibits hedging-pressure effects that Mou (2010) and subsequent authors tie to processor short-positioning ([Mou (2010), "The Reverse Soybean Crush Spread"](https://docslib.org/doc/5406596/the-reverse-soybean-crush-spread-evidence-of-the-hedging-pressure-hypothesis)).

### 1.2 Calendar spreads and the term structure

The July–November soybean spread is the bellwether of old-crop / new-crop tightness. In inverted years — where July trades above November — the market is signaling that old-crop stocks are too tight to carry until the new Midwest harvest; in contango ("full carry") years it signals abundance ([CME, "Comparing New Crop and Old Crop Risk"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2023/new-crop-vs-old-crop-risk-of-the-past-year.html); [CME, "Understanding Seasonality in Grains"](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/courses/introduction-to-grains-and-oilseeds/understanding-seasonality-in-grains); [Barchart, "Grain Spreads: July/Nov soybean spread"](https://www.barchart.com/story/news/1375153/grain-spreads-july26-nov-26-soybean-spread)). Practitioner rules of thumb: commercials fade deep carries when ending-stocks forecasts are at risk of tightening and fade deep inverses when South America is on track for a record. The variable-storage-rate logic described in Phase 01 disciplines how deep the carry can go before certificate holders find it profitable to take delivery and re-issue rather than roll ([Irwin, Garcia, Good & Kunda (2009), SSRN 1392380](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1392380)).

Seasonal-spread practitioners — Moore Research and Jake Bernstein most prominently — have published composite studies showing a statistically reliable tendency for July/November to weaken into U.S. spring and strengthen into summer weather windows ([Jake Bernstein, *Seasonal Futures Spreads* (Wiley, 1990)](https://www.abebooks.com/9780471502142/Jake-Bernsteins-Seasonal-Futures-Spreads-0471502146/plp); [SeasonAlgo, "Why trade seasonality?"](https://www.seasonalgo.com/seasonality)). Farmdoc and Oklahoma Farm Report publish contrarian critiques — over the last fifteen years soybean cash was higher at harvest than at spring in only three (2015, 2019, 2024), and only ~20% of years conform cleanly to the composite path ([Farmdoc Daily, "Seasonal Price Rally in Soybeans"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2020/06/seasonal-price-rally-in-soybeans.html); [Oklahoma Farm Report, "Price Seasonality: What the Pattern Shows"](https://www.oklahomafarmreport.com/2026/02/04/price-seasonality-what-the-pattern-shows/)).

### 1.3 Inter-market ratios: beans/corn, meal/oil, oilshare

The *new-crop bean/corn ratio* (November ZS ÷ December ZC) is watched into spring planting. Published rule of thumb, repeated across Farm Progress, Michigan State Extension, and Iowa State Ag Decision Maker, is that a ratio above ~2.5 favors soybean acreage, below ~2.2 favors corn; the long-run average sits near 2.3–2.45 ([FarmProgress, "Neutral price ratio guides 2026 crop choices"](https://www.farmprogress.com/crops/soybean-to-corn-price-ratio-offers-neutral-signal-for-2026-planting-decisions-across-midwest-farms); [Southern Ag Today, "A Way-Too-Early Soybean-to-Corn Price Ratio Analysis"](https://southernagtoday.org/2024/01/29/a-way-too-early-soybean-to-corn-price-ratio-analysis-price-implications-and-planting-decisions/); [Michigan State Extension](https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/the-corn-soybean-ratio-and-its-potential-impact-on-farm-profits); [Iowa State Ag Decision Maker](https://www.extension.iastate.edu/agdm/crops/html/a2-40.html)). Discretionary specs bet on acreage revisions by fading the ratio when it reaches an extreme that, if sustained, would imply acreage shifts the USDA June Acreage report is unlikely to validate.

The *oilshare* — ZL's share of combined product value — has its own playbook. The CME's 2024/25 oilshare-futures launch consolidated what hedgers had been executing as a 5-oil / 3-meal ratio ([CME, "Soybean Oilshare: Exploring the Benefits of a Consolidated Relative Value Instrument"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2025/soybean-oilshare-exploring-the-benefits-of-a-consolidated-relative-value-instrument.html); [CME, "An Introduction to Soybean Oilshare Futures and Options"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2025/an-introduction-to-soybean-oilshare-futures-and-options.html); [CME, "Why Soybean Oilshare Is Not Linear"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2025/why-soybean-oilshare-is-not-linear.html)). With oilshare periodically punching through 45–50% on renewable-diesel demand, the policy-binary is now a first-order driver of oilshare direction — "long oilshare is long RFS," as StoneX and farmdoc have framed it ([Farmdoc Daily, "Volatile Biofuels Demand is Reshaping the Price Relationship between Soybean and Palm Oils"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2025/12/volatile-biofuels-demand-is-reshaping-the-price-relationship-between-soybean-and-palm-oils.html); [Holland & Knight, "EPA Boosts Biofuel Mandates in Final Renewable Fuel Standard Rule for 2026-2027"](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/epa-boosts-biofuel-mandates-in-final-renewable-fuel-standard-rule); [AFBF, "EPA's Renewable Fuel Proposal Boosts Volumes"](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/epas-renewable-fuel-proposal-boosts-volumes)).

## 2. Report-driven trading

The USDA calendar is the scheduled information flow — WASDE (monthly, 12:00 ET), Crop Progress (weekly Mondays 16:00 ET, April–November), weekly Export Sales (Thursday 08:30 ET), the quarterly Grain Stocks reports (last business day of Jan/Mar/Jun/Sep, 12:00 ET), March Prospective Plantings and June Acreage ([USDA FAS, "Export Sales Reporting Program"](https://www.fas.usda.gov/programs/export-sales-reporting-program); [USDA NASS, "Grain Stocks Survey"](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Surveys/Guide_to_NASS_Surveys/Off-Farm_Grain_Stocks/index.php); [CME, "Understanding Major USDA Reports"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2024/understanding-major-usda-reports.html)).

**Pre-positioning and the whisper.** Practitioner coverage from the Hightower Report, Allendale (Rich Nelson), Pro Farmer, and AgWeb treats the 48 hours before WASDE as a "flatten or fade" window: traders who cannot stomach limit-move gap risk reduce gross exposure; those who can price both sides of the consensus. The AgWeb tape repeatedly documents both behaviors — "Grain traders get their pre-WASDE positions in order ahead of the weekend" and "soybean traders are afraid to buy ahead of the big WASDE report in case there are any surprises" ([AgWeb, "Soybean Futures Fall on Pre-WASDE Positioning"](https://www.marketscreener.com/quote/future/WHEAT-FUTURE-ZW-CBE-C1-16218/news/Soybean-Futures-Fall-on-Pre-WASDE-Positioning-Daily-Grain-Highlights-47603465/); [AgWeb, "Grains Fade Quiet WASDE Still Trading Headlines?"](https://www.agweb.com/markets/market-analysis/grains-fade-quiet-wasde-still-trading-headlines-cattle-make-new-high-clos); [Hightower Report](https://www.hightowerreport.com)). The *consensus* comes from the Reuters and Wall Street Journal analyst surveys; the *whisper* is what sell-side desks (StoneX, Hightower, AgResource) and the specialist newsletters publish in the final hours — frequently different from the survey mean when the USDA's recent history suggests a bias (e.g., USDA's well-documented tendency to under-estimate early-season yields).

**Fade vs. follow.** The practitioner dichotomy after the release is whether a same-direction first-hour move is extending (follow) or overshooting into a reversal (fade). The empirical guide most cited on desks — CME's own research — notes WASDE typically *reduces* implied-vol uncertainty about 70% of the time; in that majority case, a post-report expansion that stalls inside the first hour is the canonical fade setup ([CME, "Understanding Major USDA Reports"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2024/understanding-major-usda-reports.html)). Farmdoc Daily's September 2025 post-mortem on the Grain Stocks "shock" — soybean stocks came in "modestly tighter" but corn stocks nearly 200 million bushels above the analyst average — illustrates a classic fade failure when the number is a genuine regime change rather than statistical noise ([Farmdoc Daily, "Corn and Soybean Markets Shrug Off Bearish USDA Figures"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2025/09/corn-and-soybean-markets-shrug-off-bearish-usda-figures.html); [AgWeb, "USDA Quarterly Grains Stocks Report Shocks Soybean Market with Surprises in Supply"](https://www.agweb.com/news/crops/crop-production/usda-quarterly-grains-stocks-report-shocks-soybean-market-surprises)).

Pro Farmer's *Crop Tour* is the most cited independent check on USDA. Scouts count pods in a 3'×3' square from three plants per sampled field across seven states, with a published national estimate each August that traders compare line-by-line against the concurrent WASDE ([Pro Farmer, "Crop Tour Methodology"](https://www.profarmer.com/crop-tour-methodology); [Pro Farmer, "Pro Farmer releases 2025 national corn and soybean estimates"](https://www.profarmer.com/news/agriculture-news/pro-farmer-releases-2025-national-corn-and-soybean-estimates); [AgWeb, "How Pro Farmer 2025 Crop Estimates Compare and Contrast With USDA Expectations"](https://www.agweb.com/news/crops/crop-production/how-pro-farmer-2025-crop-estimates-compare-and-contrast-usda-expectati)).

## 3. Weather-driven discretionary positioning

**Northern Hemisphere pod-fill.** Midwest June–August is the canonical weather window. CME's own white papers — "Vol is High by the Fourth of July" and "Weather Markets in Grain Futures" — document the seasonal vol-pump and show it clustered around the U.S. ridge-pattern risk window ([CME, "Vol is High by the Fourth of July"](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/whitepapers/vol-is-high-by-the-fourth-of-july.html); [CME, "Weather Markets in Grain Futures"](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/whitepapers/weather-markets-in-grain-futures.html)). The discretionary play — ascribed on the tape to AgResource's Dan Basse, the Hightower Report, and StoneX — is to overlay the two-week GFS/ECMWF consensus against NASS state crop-condition ratings each Monday, then size exposure into forecasted heat ridges rather than hold it through the whole window. Basse frames the setup as global stock/use tightness combined with weather optionality: "world exporter grain stock/use ratios are historically tight, and outside of large U.S. corn stocks, the world does not sit on an overabundance of grain" ([World Grain, "A conversation with market analyst Dan Basse"](https://www.world-grain.com/articles/17841-a-conversation-with-market-analyst-dan-basse); [AgWeb, "Dan Basse: Prepare For Abrupt And Sizable Price Swings in 2024"](https://www.agweb.com/markets/market-outlooks/dan-basse-prepare-abrupt-and-sizable-price-swings-2024)).

**ENSO playbook.** La Niña correlates with dryness in southern Brazil and Argentina; El Niño typically benefits South American yields but can spoil U.S. conditions via Gulf-moisture surplus ([CME, "How Will La Niña Impact Ag Markets"](https://www.cmegroup.com/insights/economic-research/2022/how-will-la-nina-impact-ag-markets.html); [Farmdoc Daily, "Third Consecutive La Niña? What to Expect from Soybean Yields in the United States, Brazil and Argentina"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2022/05/third-consecutive-la-nina-what-to-expect-from-soybean-yields-in-the-united-states-brazil-and-argentina.html); [CropProphet, "La Niña's Impact on Argentina Crop Yields"](https://www.cropprophet.com/argentina-crop-impact-of-la-nina/)). The 2021–22 La Niña produced a $3/bu rally in soymeal off Argentine drought — a blueprint many desks reference when Argentina enters pod-fill in late January–February ([S&P Global, "Commodities markets brace for La Nina weather impacts"](https://www.spglobal.com/commodity-insights/en/news-research/latest-news/metals/071624-feature-commodities-markets-brace-for-la-nina-weather-impacts)).

**South American summer window.** January–February is the high-information window for Brazil's Center-West and Rio Grande do Sul and for Argentina. Practitioners watch CONAB, AgRural, IMEA, and Agroconsult crop-tour estimates against weekly private commentary; the "soy_and_corn.com" monthly crop calendar and Argus Brazil planting-calendar pieces are the most cited public primers ([Argus Media, "Brazil sets planting calendar for 2024-25 soybean crop"](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2568730-brazil-sets-planting-calendar-for-2024-25-soybean-crop); [USDA FAS IPAD "Brazil Crop Travel — Commodity Intelligence Report"](https://ipad.fas.usda.gov/highlights/2025/03/Brazil/index.pdf); [Farmdoc Daily, "Record Soybean Harvest in South America and Favorable Outlook for Exports"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2025/03/record-soybean-harvest-in-south-america-and-favorable-outlook-for-exports.html); [SoybeansAndCorn.com, "Annual Month-By-Month Crop Production Cycle in Brazil"](https://www.soybeansandcorn.com/articles/10088/)).

## 4. Basis trading: country vs. export, barges, and river conditions

Basis trading is the cash-side mirror of the futures game. Country elevators take farmer flow, basis-trade short futures against long cash, and earn the basis appreciation from harvest lows into the spring squeeze ([Wisconsin Extension, "Understanding Basis in Grain Marketing"](https://farms.extension.wisc.edu/articles/understanding-basis-in-grain-marketing/); [StoneX, "Basics of Grain Basis Trading"](https://futures.stonex.com/blog/basics-of-grain-basis-trading-long-the-basis); [Iowa State Ag Decision Maker, "Corn and Soybean Price Basis"](https://www.extension.iastate.edu/agdm/crops/html/a2-40.html)). At harvest, heavy farmer selling compresses cash while futures hold up, so basis blows out; post-harvest, basis narrows as inventories draw and logistics constraints emerge ([CoBank, "Slow Farmer Selling Frustrates Elevators' Acquisition of Corn and Soybeans"](https://www.cobank.com/knowledge-exchange/grain-and-farm-supply/slow-farmer-selling-frustrates-elevators-acquisition-of-corn-and-soybeans)).

Export basis at Gulf and PNW terminals tracks global demand against barge and rail logistics. The 2022–2024 low-water episodes on the Mississippi and Illinois rivers — CME and CoBank both covered — pushed Memphis and St. Louis basis to their weakest levels since 2019, as barge freight ballooned and elevators absorbed the differential ([CME OpenMarkets, "Low River Levels are Creating Higher Costs for Farmers"](https://www.cmegroup.com/openmarkets/agriculture/2024/Low-River-Levels-are-Creating-Higher-Costs-for-Farmers.html); [CoBank, "Grain Logistics Outlook: Record Crop Meets Trade Uncertainty"](https://www.cobank.com/web/cobank/knowledge-exchange/grain-and-farm-supply/grain-logistics-outlook-record-crop-meets-trade-uncertainty); [FarmTalk, "When barge traffic slows, soybean basis suffers"](https://www.farmtalknews.com/news/when-barge-traffic-slows-soybean-basis-suffers/article_83cd3b62-ffb8-11ed-9878-f7eb17e86958.html); [Talk Business & Politics, "Trade wars, low river levels reducing soybean prices"](https://talkbusiness.net/2025/09/trade-wars-low-river-levels-reducing-soybean-prices/)). A country-elevator basis trader reads USACE lock tonnage, DTN river-levels feed, and Illinois Waterway status daily. The Pro Farmer / Soy Transportation Coalition data show that ~75% of U.S. beans move through a country elevator before reaching barge or rail ([Soy Transportation Coalition, "Farm to Market Pipeline Distribution at Harvest"](https://www.soytransportation.org/KeyFindings/SoybeanFarmToMarketPipeline.pdf); [USDA AMS, "Export Elevator Overview"](https://www.ams.usda.gov/resources/export-elevator-overview)). Basis desks turn that into a simple two-legged playbook: *long basis* (buy cash, short futures) when barge freight is expected to fall (wet year, strong river); *short basis* when freight will rise (drought, low-water summer).

## 5. Anticipating hedging flows

**Farmer selling at harvest.** The canonical pattern: farmers sell combine-run beans in October–November, compressing both outright price and basis. Agweb and Pro Farmer repeatedly document the pattern ("Soybeans saw hedge pressure with a dry open weekend for harvest across the Midwest"; "National average basis reached 80.5 cents under November futures in September 2025") ([AgWeb, "Grains See Profit Taking as Harvest Expands"](https://www.agweb.com/markets/market-analysis/grains-see-profit-taking-harvest-expands-soybean-basis-worst-2019); [Farm Progress, "Sell grain off the combine?"](https://www.farmprogress.com/commentary/sell-grain-off-the-combine-)). The speculator response is to fade the harvest low once farmer flow slows — Iowa State's five-year average basis of $0.76/bu under November futures is the cited benchmark ([Iowa State Ag Decision Maker](https://www.extension.iastate.edu/agdm/crops/html/a2-40.html)).

**Crusher fixing of meal and oil.** Crushers sell product forward into feed-deal and biofuel-offtake flows, then buy back whenever their book becomes unbalanced. Desk lore: "when crush margins are fat, crushers sell meal/oil to the trade and bid beans hard" — visible in the tape as basis firming and crush spread pay-down ([FarmProgress, "High crush margins drive rapid expansion"](https://www.farmprogress.com/soybean/high-crush-margins-drive-rapid-expansion); [FarmProgress, "Will soy crush demand tick up?"](https://www.farmprogress.com/commentary/will-soy-crush-demand-tick-up-)).

**Producer and index rolls.** CTAs and commercials alike try to front-run the Goldman roll window — days five through nine of the month preceding contract expiration — when 20%/day of S&P GSCI-indexed open interest moves from the front to the next-out ([Goldman Roll — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldman_roll); [Irwin, Sanders & Yan, "The Order Flow Cost of Index Rolling in Commodity Futures Markets"](https://scotthirwin.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/Irwin_Sanders_Yan_AEPP_All.pdf); [CFTC Yu, "Limits to Arbitrage and Commodity Index Investment: Front-Running the Goldman Roll"](https://www.cftc.gov/sites/default/files/idc/groups/public/@swaps/documents/file/plstudy_33_yu.pdf)). In soybeans the effect is smaller than in crude but still tradeable around the five major roll months the GSCI prescribes.

## 6. Positioning / COT-based discretion

Since 2009 the CFTC publishes the *Disaggregated* Commitments of Traders report alongside the Legacy report, splitting Producer/Merchant/Processor/User (commercials), Swap Dealers, Managed Money, and Other Reportables ([CFTC, "Commitments of Traders"](https://www.cftc.gov/MarketReports/CommitmentsofTraders/index.htm); [CFTC, "Disaggregated Explanatory Notes"](https://www.cftc.gov/MarketReports/CommitmentsofTraders/DisaggregatedExplanatoryNotes/index.htm)). The practitioner framework, published widely on the CQG and Barchart platforms, is that *extreme* Managed Money net longs or shorts are typically contrarian signals when they pair with stalling price action ([CQG Primer, "Analyze Commitment of Traders with an Oscillator"](https://news.cqg.com/workspaces/2024/07/cqg-primer-analyze-commitment-traders-cot-oscillator); [Barchart, "Commitments of Traders Charts"](https://www.barchart.com/futures/commitment-of-traders); [Pro Farmer, "From net short to massive longs: How the CFTC blackout masked the soybean rally"](https://www.profarmer.com/news/agriculture-news/how-cot-reports-changed-over-delayed-reporting-period)).

Practitioner reading rules, drawn from Pro Farmer's and StoneX's commentary and academic work by Sanders, Irwin, and Merrin: (i) inflection points are more reliable when Managed Money flips from net short to net long (and vice versa) coincident with a momentum signal on price; (ii) *absolute* extreme longs (>200,000 contracts in ZS) preceded 2021 and 2025 tops in recent memory but similar readings persisted for months in the 2012 drought; (iii) commercial positioning is structurally short, so "commercials are building length" is a more informative tape than "specs are long" ([ScienceDirect, "Four Commitments of Traders Reports puzzles, revisited"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2405851324000084)).

## 7. Rumor and headline trading

Grain markets run on a daily headline cadence: USDA daily flash-sale announcements (>100,000 MT to a single destination in a single day), weekly Thursday export sales, Chinese state-buyer rumors, biofuel-policy leaks, and tariff tape. Jack Schwager's *Market Wizards* captures the playbook psychology — Bruce Kovner's famous soybean loss, in which he correctly anticipated tight supply and took a position to $45,000 gain on a $4,000 stake, then abandoned his hedge "in a moment of insanity" and liquidated at $22,000, is the canonical lesson on why even a correct thesis needs execution discipline around rumor-tape vol ([Schwager (1989), *Market Wizards*, ch. on Bruce Kovner](https://www.valueplays.net/wp-content/uploads/41775536-Market-Wizards.pdf); [TurtleTrader profile on Kovner](https://www.turtletrader.com/trader-kovner/); [QuantifiedStrategies, "Bruce Kovner — Trade Small, Manage Risk"](https://www.quantifiedstrategies.com/bruce-kovner/)).

The modern tape's most reliable setup is the "buy the rumor, sell the fact" pattern on Chinese purchases. AgWeb, Brownfield, and Pro Farmer all documented the October 2025 framework-deal and November 2025 flash-sale sequence in which prices faded after each confirmation — "on Monday, traders bought the rumor China was purchasing U.S. soybeans, but on Tuesday and Wednesday, they sold the facts" ([Brownfield Ag News, "Soybeans continue to sell the fact on export sales to China"](https://www.brownfieldagnews.com/market-news/soybeans-continue-to-sell-the-fact-on-export-sales-to-china/); [Pro Farmer, "Cutting Through the Confusion: White House Confirms Trade Agreement With China on Soybeans"](https://www.profarmer.com/news/agriculture-news/cutting-through-confusion); [AgWeb, "Soybeans Fade Confirmation of Big China Purchases"](https://www.agweb.com/markets/market-analysis/soybeans-fade-confirmation-big-china-purchases-corn-tries-rally-alone); [Ag Update, "China rumors driving soybean market"](https://agupdate.com/farmandranchguide/markets/crop/article_25dff50d-24c0-4d26-bcfc-cbe6c0c099e9.html)). Biofuel-policy trades work the same way — the 2023–2026 RFS rulemaking produced two sharp oilshare rallies (2023 RVO proposal, 2025/26 "Set 2" rule) where positioning chased each new leak ([AFBF, "EPA's Renewable Fuel Proposal Boosts Volumes"](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/epas-renewable-fuel-proposal-boosts-volumes); [AgWeb, "Historic RFS Mandates Drive 2 Billion-Gallon Expansion"](https://www.agweb.com/news/crops/soybeans/new-biofuel-boom-historic-rfs-mandates-drive-2-billion-gallon-expansion); [EIA, "Biomass-based diesel and ethanol compliance credit prices decline 45% from start of year"](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=61463)).

## 8. Seasonal discretionary plays and the practitioner dispute

The most cited seasonal soybean patterns are: the *February break* (price fatigue as U.S. old crop competes with Brazilian new crop), the *summer weather rally* (June–July pop on ridge-pattern risk), and the *harvest low* (October–November). Jake Bernstein's *Seasonal Futures Spreads* and Moore Research's published composites formalize them; SeasonAlgo and CME's own primer re-state them ([Jake Bernstein, *Seasonal Futures Spreads*](https://www.abebooks.com/9780471502142/Jake-Bernsteins-Seasonal-Futures-Spreads-0471502146/plp); [SeasonAlgo, "Seasonality"](https://www.seasonalgo.com/seasonality); [CME, "Understanding Seasonality in Grains"](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/courses/introduction-to-grains-and-oilseeds/understanding-seasonality-in-grains); [Wisconsin Extension, "Seasonal Grain Price Patterns"](https://farms.extension.wisc.edu/articles/seasonal-grain-price-patterns/)).

Academic critiques — farmdoc's Scott Irwin and Joe Janzen most prominently — warn that the composite hides massive dispersion and that in the last 15 years soybean cash was higher at harvest than in spring only three times ([Farmdoc Daily, "Seasonal Price Rally in Soybeans"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2020/06/seasonal-price-rally-in-soybeans.html); [Oklahoma Farm Report, "Price Seasonality: What the Pattern Shows"](https://www.oklahomafarmreport.com/2026/02/04/price-seasonality-what-the-pattern-shows/); [University of Kentucky Ag Econ, "Drivers of Commodity Prices: Seasonality"](https://agecon.mgcafe.uky.edu/drivers-commodity-prices-seasonality)). The documented practitioner disagreement is therefore real: seasonal composites remain defensible as *vol regimes* (vol is reliably higher into July 4 and into March Prospective Plantings) but indefensible as standalone *directional* signals. Peter Brandt's written critique of seasonality-as-edge ("trade identification, or signaling, is far down the list of important aspects of market speculation") is the most cited practitioner rejection of seasonal-as-strategy ([Peter Brandt, "The Four Key Pillars of Factor"](https://www.peterlbrandt.com/knowledge-center/four-key-pillars-factor/); [Peter Brandt, "Always Consider Risk Management"](https://www.peterlbrandt.com/risk-management/)).

Each strategy family uses a structured entry trigger, sizing rule, invalidation, horizon, and preferred expression. The following table condenses the practitioner literature into a comparable grid.

On sizing specifically, the practitioner literature converges on a small number of dominant rules. **Peter Brandt** risks ≤0.5–1% of nominal capital per trade, with position size backed out from the distance to his "Last Day Rule" stop; he accepts a ~30% win rate because average winner / average loser is ~3:1 ([Peter Brandt, "Risk Management in Trading"](https://www.peterlbrandt.com/risk-management-trading/); [Peter Brandt, "Always Consider Risk Management"](https://www.peterlbrandt.com/risk-management/); [Financial Wisdom TV, "Diary of a Professional Trader"](https://www.financialwisdomtv.com/post/diary-of-a-professional-trader-by-peter-brandt)). **Richard Dennis's Turtle system** sizes at 1 "unit" where a unit is the notional dollar amount at which a 1 × N move (where N ≈ 20-day ATR) equals 1% of equity; pyramiding is capped at 4 units per market and 6 per closely-correlated group ([Covel, *The Complete TurtleTrader*](https://www.amazon.com/Complete-TurtleTrader-Investors-Overnight-Millionaires/dp/0061241717); [HowToTrade, "The Turtle Trading strategy" PDF](https://howtotrade.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/The-Turtle-Trading-strategy.pdf)). **Jerry Parker** sizes on ATR with a minimum-ATR floor to prevent outsized positions when volatility compresses, and allocates a 25% risk-budget slice to commodities within a four-asset-class framework ([Top Traders Unplugged, Jerry Parker guest page](https://www.toptradersunplugged.com/guests/jerry-parker/); [Mutiny Fund podcast with Jerry Parker](https://mutinyfund.com/jerry-parker/); [The Hedge Fund Journal, "Chesapeake Capital's Jerry Parker"](https://thehedgefundjournal.com/chesapeake-capitals-jerry-parker/)). **Linda Raschke's** swing-trading rules — from *Street Smarts* and her public teaching — size positions by the risk-to-stop in ticks and pair them with the *Holy Grail* (ADX>30 + pullback to 20-EMA) and *Anti* (bull/bear flag after an impulse, with MACD 3-10-16 confirmation) entries ([Raschke & Connors, *Street Smarts*](https://www.amazon.com/Street-Smarts-Probability-Short-Term-Strategies/dp/0965046109); [Traders Mastermind, "Linda Raschke Trading Strategy"](https://tradersmastermind.com/linda-raschke-trading-strategy/); [New Trader U, "Market Wizard Linda Raschke Trading Strategy"](https://www.newtraderu.com/2022/08/25/market-wizard-linda-raschke-trading-strategy/)). **Gary Bielfeldt**, profiled by Schwager as the Peoria soybean-and-bond specialist, refused diversification on principle: "he confined himself to trading a single corn contract and eventually expanded to the soybean complex," sizing up only after the trend had proved itself ([Schwager, *Market Wizards*, "Yes, They Do Trade T-Bonds in Peoria"](https://www.amazon.com/Market-Wizards-Interviews-Aggressive-Bielfeldt/dp/B00MXI3R9I); ## 10. Failure modes and documented blowups

**2012 drought-rally fades.** November soybeans rallied from a $12.45 low on June 4, 2012 to a $17.89 all-time high on September 4, with July 2013 peaking $3.85 above its June low on September 14, 2012 ([Farmdoc Daily, "Corn and Soybean Prices Continue to Retrace 2012 Drought Rally"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2013/05/corn-soybean-prices-retrace-2012-rally.html); [DTN, "A Look at Past Soybean Peaks"](https://www.dtnpf.com/agriculture/web/ag/news/article/2021/01/19/look-past-soybean-peaks); [CNN Money, "Corn, soybean prices shoot up as drought worsens"](https://money.cnn.com/2012/07/19/investing/corn-soybean-prices/index.htm)). Traders who faded the rally on the "short-crop" seasonal template (price peaks at harvest, fades into the marketing year) were right on the path but often wrong on timing; several documented CTA drawdowns came from fading too early in July–August 2012 and getting stopped out before the September peak.

**2018 tariff gap.** On April 4, 2018, Beijing announced a 25% import-tariff list targeting U.S. soybeans; May ZS gapped ~40¢ lower intraday before partially recovering ([Farmdoc Daily, "Trade Timeline and Corn and Soybean Prices"](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2018/07/trade-timeline-and-corn-and-soybean-prices.html); [Yeutter Institute, "How Has China Responded to the Tariffs it Placed on American Soybeans?"](https://yeutter-institute.unl.edu/trade-knowledge-hub/student-briefing-papers/how-has-china-responded-tariffs-it-placed-american/); [PIIE, "Trump, China, and Tariffs: From Soybeans to Semiconductors"](https://www.piie.com/blogs/trade-and-investment-policy-watch/2018/trump-china-and-tariffs-soybeans-semiconductors); [Kansas City Fed, "Reshuffling in Soybean Markets following Chinese Tariffs"](https://www.kansascityfed.org/documents/6891/reshuffling-soybean-markets-v105n1cowley.pdf)). Farmers and commercials holding basis-long positions without headline-risk hedges absorbed the shock directly. Managed-money specs riding a pre-tariff long into April 2018 illustrate the perennial failure mode — size that is fine in a data-driven tape becomes fatal in a headline-driven one.

**2018 OptionSellers.com (adjacent, cautionary).** James Cordier's short-strangle fund — targeting energy, not grain — blew up $150 million on a November 2018 nat-gas spike. The lesson generalizes to grain short-gamma desks: the structural risk is not a single event but *the combination of negative gamma, a headline-sensitive market, and insufficient wing cover* ([Early Retirement Now, "The OptionSellers.com debacle"](https://earlyretirementnow.com/2018/12/18/the-optionsellers-debacle/); [Wolf Street, "US Natural Gas Plunges, Unwinds Spike that Blew Up Hedge Fund"](https://wolfstreet.com/2018/12/17/us-natural-gas-plunges-unwinds-spike-that-blew-up-hedge-fund/); [SteadyOptions, "James Cordier: Another Options Selling Firm Goes Bust"](https://steadyoptions.com/articles/james-cordier-another-options-selling-firm-goes-bust-r429/)). Grain short-strangle sellers into USDA reports or South American weather windows face the same structural payoff.

**2022 war-driven vol.** Russia's February 2022 invasion of Ukraine locked palm, sunflower, and soy oil limit-up for multiple sessions; soybean oil averaged $1,957/t in March 2022 versus $765/t in 2019 ([USDA ERS, "Examining Record Soybean Oil Prices in 2021–22"](https://www.ers.usda.gov/amber-waves/2022/december/examining-record-soybean-oil-prices-in-2021-22); [IFPRI, "The impact of the Ukraine crisis on the global vegetable oil market"](https://www.ifpri.org/blog/impact-ukraine-crisis-global-vegetable-oil-market/); [NPR, "Russia's invasion of Ukraine heats up cooking oil prices in global squeeze"](https://www.npr.org/2022/04/26/1094770364/russias-invasion-of-ukraine-heats-up-cooking-oil-prices-in-global-squeeze); [CBI, "What is the impact of the war in Ukraine on exports of vegetable oils?"](https://www.cbi.eu/market-information/grains-pulses-oilseeds/what-impact-war-ukraine-exports-vegetable-oils)). Indonesia's April 2022 palm-oil export ban amplified the spike. Practitioner lesson: correlation breaks in tail events — soybean oil stopped trading as a "feed oil" and started trading as an "energy oil," and desks running oilshare models calibrated to pre-war regimes blew through their stops.

## Key takeaways

Serious discretionary soybean trading is a handful of repeatable playbooks, each with a named practitioner genealogy and documented failure cases. Board-crush and oilshare trading rely on the physical conversion identity and the RFS policy binary. Calendar-spread and seasonal-spread work — Bernstein, Moore Research, the farmdoc critiques — is most defensible as a vol-regime framework and least defensible as a standalone directional edge. Report-driven trading revolves around the USDA calendar, the Reuters/WSJ consensus, and the whisper tape from StoneX, Hightower, AgResource, Allendale, and Pro Farmer. Weather trading is ENSO-biased and forecast-anchored, sized into the window rather than held through it. Basis trading is a cash-side mirror driven by farmer flow and river/rail logistics. Anticipating hedging flows — farmer harvest selling, crusher product fixing, index roll windows — remains one of the most durable edges in the complex. COT-based discretion needs a price-action confirmation to be reliable; absolute extremes persist. Rumor/headline trading is where Kovner-style execution discipline matters most. And the 2012, 2018, and 2022 episodes are the discipline: short-gamma size, pre-positioned basis, and mis-calibrated cross-market correlations are what turn a good thesis into a blown account.

# Phase 01 — USD/BRL FX Market Structure

This document is the foundational market-structure reference for a 16-phase research program on emerging-market FX market making, focused on the Brazilian real (BRL) leg of USD/BRL. Subsequent phases will assume this material as common ground. Wherever a primary source (Banco Central do Brasil, B3, CME Group, BIS, IMF, official law text) is available, it is cited inline; secondary and practitioner sources are used only where primary sources do not yet exist for a particular detail.

---

## 1. Spot interbank market and conventions

The Brazilian onshore interbank USD/BRL spot market is centrally cleared by **B3**'s Foreign Exchange Clearinghouse, which began operations on 22 April 2002 and clears interbank operations executed both on B3's electronic platform (PUMA) and OTC. The clearinghouse handles exclusively USD-against-BRL operations, settles on a payment-versus-payment (PvP) basis, and supports T0, T+1, and T+2 settlement; **the prevailing interbank standard is T+2**, with the BRL leg settling in dealer reserve accounts at the BCB and the USD leg settling in dealer accounts in New York ([BCB / BM&FBOVESPA Foreign Exchange Clearinghouse description](https://www.bcb.gov.br/pom/spb/ing/securitiesderivativesforeign/bmfcambioingles.asp?idpai=settlement&frame=1)). The detailed operating rules for the floor-traded USD spot session are in B3's [US Dollar Spot Operating Manual](https://www.b3.com.br/data/files/F7/02/92/DA/E5E7061099BE5706790D8AA8/us-dollar-spot-operating-manual.pdf), which fixes session start at 09:15 BRT, close at 16:00 BRT (no break), with trading-card registration accepted until 16:10 BRT.

**Quoting convention.** USD/BRL is quoted in BRL per USD (e.g., "5.0750" means 5.0750 reais per dollar). This is the universal convention in both onshore and offshore markets — there is no ambiguity of the sort that surrounds GBP/USD or EUR/GBP. The B3 USD futures contract quotes in **BRL per USD 1,000** (a quirk of the legacy BM&F contract design), so the conversion factor between spot quote and futures quote is 1000. Decimal precision in interbank spot is typically four decimals (e.g., 5.0735); on B3 futures the minimum tick is **BRL 0.5 per USD 1,000**, equivalent to 0.0005 in spot terms (see B3 [U.S. Dollar Futures product page](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/products-and-services/trading/exchange-rates/u-s-dollar-futures.htm)).

**Holiday calendar interaction.** Brazil and the US have substantially different bank-holiday calendars (Carnaval, Corpus Christi, 7 September Independence Day, 12 October Nossa Senhora Aparecida, 2 November Finados, 15 November Proclamação da República — none of these are US holidays; conversely, Memorial Day, Independence Day in July, Thanksgiving and the day after, are not Brazilian holidays). Because spot settles T+2, when one side is closed and the other open, the value date is rolled forward to the next jointly open day, lengthening the implied funding period; this is the source of recurring "casado" (basis) dislocations around long Brazilian carnival weekends. B3's published [trading calendar / holidays](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/solutions/platforms/puma-trading-system/for-members-and-traders/trading-calendar/holidays/) is the canonical reference. Interbank spreads in calm regimes are tight — typically 0.0005–0.0015 BRL between top onshore dealers — but widen by an order of magnitude during BCB intervention windows and after sharp Brent or US-rate moves; the BIS notes that in stressed EM episodes BRL spot spreads can widen 5–10x relative to G10 norms ([BIS WP 426, Kohlscheen 2013](https://www.bis.org/publ/work426.htm)).

## 2. Onshore deliverable spot vs offshore NDF

Brazil maintains a **closed capital account in the regulatory sense that offshore counterparties cannot freely deliver BRL onshore**. The real is non-deliverable for non-residents who are not registered under one of the BCB regimes; this is why offshore BRL exposure is overwhelmingly intermediated via **non-deliverable forwards (NDFs)**, settled in USD at maturity against the PTAX fixing, with NY and London as the dominant booking centers ([BIS Quarterly Review, "Non-deliverable forwards: impact of currency internationalisation"](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1612h.pdf); [BIS CGFS NDF overview, Lipscomb](https://www.bis.org/publ/cgfs22fedny5.pdf)). The BIS notes that BRL NDF turnover increased its share among EM NDFs in the period to 2016, reflecting both onshore capital controls and Brazil's depth as a tradable EM expression vehicle.

**The non-resident portfolio regime.** [BCB Resolution 4,373 of 29 September 2014](https://www.bcb.gov.br/rex/legce/Ingl/Ftp/Resolution4373.pdf) (and its predecessor, Resolution 2,689 of 2000) governs non-resident access to onshore financial and capital markets. A "4,373 account" requires the foreign investor to register with CVM (the securities regulator), appoint a Brazilian legal representative and tax representative, and engage onshore custody. Through a 4,373 vehicle, foreign investors can trade B3 equities, derivatives (including DOL/WDO and DI futures), and fixed income on the same operational footing as residents ([B3, "Trading equities, derivatives and fixed income — CMN 4.373/2014"](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/non-resident-investor/characteristics-brazilian-market/trading-equities-derivatives-and-fixed-income-cmn-4-373-2014.htm)). All FX operations go through SISBACEN, the BCB's central FX registration system, which records every authorized FX transaction daily ([SISBACEN reference paper, GWU Minerva](https://www2.gwu.edu/~ibi/minerva/Spring1999/augusto.ornelas.filho.pdf)).

**The New FX Law (Lei 14.286/2021).** Enacted 29 December 2021 and **effective 30 December 2022** ([Demarest, "Law No. 14,286"](https://www.demarest.com.br/en/law-no-14286-of-december-29-2021-new-foreign-exchange-law-will-enter-into-force-on-december-30-2022-one-year-after-its-official-publication/); [BCB official translation](https://www.bcb.gov.br/content/about/legislation_norms_docs/Law_FX_14.286.pdf)), this law consolidated the prior fragmented FX framework dating to 1933. It permits limited holding of foreign-currency accounts onshore by certain entities, modernizes non-resident-to-non-resident BRL transactions, simplifies inflow/outflow paperwork, and explicitly grants the BCB authority to expand BRL convertibility over time. Implementation regulations were issued by the BCB the same day the law took effect: Resolutions 277/2022 (FX dealing and eFX), 278/2022 (foreign loans and direct investment), 279/2022 (cross-border capital flows), 280/2022 (resident/non-resident definitions), and 281/2022 (conversion of investments) ([Pinheiro Neto overview](https://www.pinheironeto.com.br/Documents/the-new-foreign-capital-and-exchange-law.pdf); [International Bar Association](https://www.ibanet.org/The-new-Brazilian-foreign-exchange-and-foreign-capital-law)). RDE-ROF (Registro Declaratório Eletrônico — Registro de Operação Financeira) remains the registration channel for foreign loans and is a precondition for remitting interest and principal abroad ([BCB RDE-ROF reference](https://www.bcb.gov.br/Rex/legce/ingl/Circular3689Eng.asp?frame=1)).

**IOF on FX as of 2026.** This is volatile and must be checked for the current state. The famous 6% IOF on portfolio inflows (October 2009 to June 2013) is long abolished. After the New FX Law, IOF-FX rates were reduced in stages with a stated intent to reach 0% on most outbound FX by 2029. In **May 2025**, Decree 12,466/2025 sharply increased IOF-FX rates on credit and FX operations; the Supreme Federal Court (STF) and Congress overturned most of those increases in July 2025. Following the post-July reset, **inbound FX (export proceeds, foreign-investment inflows, repurchase) is taxed at 0.38%**; **outbound FX is generally 3.5%, with foreign investment outflows at 1.10% and return of foreign investment at 0%** following Decree 12,499/2025 ([EY tax alert on Decree 12,466 changes](https://www.ey.com/en_gl/technical/tax-alerts/brazilian-government-announces-substantial-tax-changes-affecting-interest-on-net-equity-financial-investments-betting-operations-and-iof-regulations); [BDO summary of overturned rates](https://www.bdo.global/en-gb/insights/tax/indirect-tax/brazil-changes-to-financial-transactions-tax-rates-overturned-but-other-measures-retained); [International Tax Review IOF guide](https://www.internationaltaxreview.com/article/2a68rfy5bw2ycq128bf1b/brazil-understanding-the-tax-on-financial-operations-iof)). For market-making purposes the salient point is that **non-resident portfolio inflows under 4,373 attract a 0% IOF rate today on equity and a small 0.38% on most relevant FX legs**, but specific cross-border transfers, trade flows, and outbound investments retain non-trivial IOF exposure.

## 3. CME BRL futures (6L) and BRL options

[CME Group's Brazilian Real / U.S. Dollar futures (CME Globex code 6L; clearing code BR)](https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/fx/emerging-market/brazilian-real.contractSpecs.html) are the principal **offshore** hedging venue for non-residents who cannot easily access B3. Contract size is **BRL 100,000** (the popular description "500,000 BRL" appearing in some third-party sources predates a contract redesign; the current rulebook is unambiguous). Quotation is in **USD per BRL** (i.e., the reciprocal of the onshore BRL/USD convention) — this is the structural source of "European" vs "American" terms confusion when comparing 6L to DOL on B3. Minimum price fluctuation is **USD 0.00005 per BRL**, equivalent to USD 5.00 per contract. Trading runs nearly 24 hours on Globex, Sunday 17:00 CT to Friday 16:00 CT, with a daily 16:00–17:00 CT maintenance halt.

**Settlement.** The 6L is **financially settled** against the BCB PTAX fixing. The last trading day is the last business day of the month immediately preceding the contract month at 09:15 CT, on which the BCB is scheduled to publish its end-of-month "Commercial exchange rate for Brazilian reais per U.S. dollar for cash delivery" (PTAX). The final settlement price is the reciprocal of that PTAX rate ([CME Rulebook Chapter 257](https://www.cmegroup.com/rulebook/CME/III/250/257/257.pdf)). CME also lists **BRL options** on 6L and a "Brazilian Real Options" series with European-style exercise and end-of-month expiry aligned to the futures.

**Liquidity profile.** 6L liquidity is concentrated in the front month; back-month volume is typically two orders of magnitude lighter. Open interest typically peaks in the calendar quarter ahead of major BRL macro catalysts (COPOM meetings, US payrolls). For an offshore market maker who cannot register a 4,373 account, 6L is the de-facto hedging venue against an offshore NDF book; the structural problem is that 6L final settlement is end-of-month while NDFs settle on each trade's individual fixing date, generating residual basis risk that has to be managed via futures roll calendars ([CME Brazilian Real calendar](https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/fx/emerging-market/brazilian-real.calendar.html); [CME "Accessing Brazil" guide](https://www.cmegroup.com/trading/files/accessing-brazil.pdf)).

## 4. B3 (formerly BM&FBovespa) FX complex

B3 is the deepest BRL futures venue in the world by volume. The BIS notes that "a large share of trading in the Brazilian real ... is done via exchange-traded derivatives" rather than OTC, which is why BRL appears under-represented in BIS Triennial OTC turnover figures ([BIS Triennial 2022 reporting guidelines](https://www.bis.org/statistics/triennialrep/2022survey_guidelinesturnover.pdf)). The exchange's own derivatives volume — across all asset classes — was 8.3 billion contracts in 2023, the world's second-largest derivatives complex behind India's NSE.

**DOL — full-size USD futures.** Per the B3 contract sheet ([U.S. Dollar Futures](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/products-and-services/trading/exchange-rates/u-s-dollar-futures.htm)): ticker **DOL**, contract size **USD 50,000**, quotation **BRL per USD 1,000**, tick size **BRL 0.5 per USD 1,000**, round lot 5 contracts, all months listed, **expiration on the first business day of the contract month**, last trading day the immediately preceding business day, cash settlement against PTAX. DOLFUT plus month/year code (e.g., DOLM26 for June 2026) is the trading symbol. Settlement specifically uses the prior-day PTAX (PTAX D-1 of expiration day), which is itself the average of four BCB dealer-poll windows that day.

**WDO — mini USD futures.** Ticker **WDO**, contract size 20% of DOL, i.e., **USD 10,000**, same quotation convention, BRL 10.00 per point in tick terms ([B3 Mini U.S. Dollar Futures](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/products-and-services/trading/exchange-rates/mini-u-s-dollar-futures.htm); [B3 Educação Mini Dólar](https://edu.b3.com.br/en/w/mini-dolar)). WDO has become one of the highest-volume mini-FX futures globally, driven by the explosion of Brazilian retail since 2018 ([Brazil Stock Guide / B3 retail figures](https://brazilstockguide.com/insights/b3-2026-strategy-derivatives-retail-growth/)). The retail CPF count on B3 grew from 813,000 in December 2018 to over 5 million by 2023.

**DR1 — rolling spot future.** A relatively recent product: an end-of-day rolling cash-settled USD/BRL future that obviates the need to roll DOL contracts every month. Liquidity is far thinner than DOL/WDO but the contract is used by some passive hedgers.

**DI futures and the cupom cambial complex.** B3's interest-rate complex interacts with FX through two key contracts: the **DI1** (one-day interbank deposit future, BRL nominal short rate) and the **DDI** (DI x USD spread future). DDI quotes the **cupom cambial sujo** — the USD-denominated rate implied by the difference between BRL nominal rates and USD/BRL forward points, contaminated by the previous day's PTAX move ([B3 DDI contract spec](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/products-and-services/trading/interest-rates/di-x-u-s-dollar-spread-futures.htm); [B3 DDI public information PDF](https://www.b3.com.br/lumis/portal/file/fileDownload.jsp?fileId=8A828D2951C9C37701522244DD313109)). The **FRC** (Forward Rate Agreement on cupom cambial), introduced in January 2001, is the **clean** cupom cambial: by combining a long DDI at maturity t2 with a short DDI at t1, FRC isolates the forward USD rate between t1 and t2 free of the prior day's exchange-rate noise ([Clube de Finanças, "Mercado Futuro de Cupom Cambial (DDI e FRC)"](https://clubedefinancas.com.br/materias/mercado-futuro-de-cupom-cambial-ddi-e-frc/)). The cupom cambial curve is the BRL-onshore expression of USD funding cost and is the link between local BRL rates and offshore USD rates.

**The casado.** The "casado" (married) trade is the simultaneous sale of USD spot and purchase of DOL futures (or vice versa) at a quoted spread that is, in essence, the BRL-onshore-USD-funding rate plus the FX clearinghouse settlement convention. It is the workhorse arbitrage that links B3 spot to B3 DOL and is the mechanism by which BCB spot intervention transmits to the curve. Forward Points contracts (FRP0 trading 09:00–13:00 BRT and FRP1 09:00–18:00 BRT) are the structured-transaction wrapper used to lock the casado in pre-defined size ([B3 "Forward Points on US Dollar Futures" structured transaction PDF](https://www.b3.com.br/lumis/portal/file/fileDownload.jsp?fileId=8A828D2951C9C3770152222C27D114C8)).

## 5. BCB intervention regime

The BCB operates a flexible-exchange-rate regime since 1999 but actively intervenes via several distinct instruments. The reaction function is published in regular language in the **Relatório de Política Monetária** (renamed from "Relatório de Inflação" by Decree 12.079/2024); the September 2025 issue is representative of current language ([BCB Monetary Policy Report, Sept 2025](https://www.bcb.gov.br/content/ri/inflationreport/202509/rpm202509i.pdf)). The IMF's [2025 Article IV Brazil report](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2025/english/1braea2025001-source-pdf.pdf) characterizes the BCB stance as "intervention to address episodes of higher risk premia when FX liquidity becomes shallow, without substituting for warranted adjustment of macroeconomic policies."

**Spot intervention auctions.** The BCB conducts two-way spot auctions, *leilão de venda à vista* (sells USD into the market) and *leilão de compra à vista* (buys). These typically happen during liquidity shortages or one-way client flow; the BCB sold USD 21.6 billion in spot auctions in December 2024 (≈6% of reserves) and had not intervened in spot through late June 2025 per the IMF ([IMF 2025 Article IV](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2025/english/1braea2025001-source-pdf.pdf)).

**Line auctions (leilão de linha).** USD repo auctions in which the BCB lends USD to dealers against a future repurchase commitment. Functionally similar to a Fed term repo facility but in dollars. Used to provide USD liquidity without permanently shrinking the FX reserve stock.

**Traditional swap auctions (swap cambial tradicional).** This is the workhorse instrument from 2013 onward. Technically a domestic non-deliverable forward (DNDF) — a BRL-settled forward in which the BCB is short USD/long the cupom cambial — and clears on B3 under code SCC. Selling traditional swaps is economically equivalent to selling USD forward; the BCB pays the counterparty the USD return and receives the cupom cambial coupon. Garcia and Volpon ([Semantic Scholar reference](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/DNDFs:a-more-efficient-way-to-intervene-in-FX-Garcia-Volpon/8d2c0e57e53b87f83d8ba8a6f837625ce34a3275/figure/1)) argue that DNDFs are more efficient than spot intervention: they compress forward points without using FX reserves, and they incentivize commercial banks to bring USD onshore via the casado trade.

**Reverse swap auctions (swap reverso).** The BCB takes the opposite side, becoming long USD forward — used to drain hedging supply when the real is appreciating and the curve is too steep on the USD-funding side.

**Cupom cambial intervention.** In addition to outright swaps the BCB occasionally tweaks the cupom cambial directly via the FRC market.

**Magnitude.** The peak BCB swap book reached approximately **USD 110 billion at end-March 2015** and approached USD 120 billion at the program's height — close to 7% of Brazilian GDP and 30% of FX reserves at that moment ([Macalos, Boeckler-Stiftung working paper](https://www.boeckler.de/pdf/v_2017_11_10_macalos.pdf); [IMF WP 17/11, "Relative Effectiveness of Spot and Derivatives Based Interventions"](https://www.imf.org/-/media/Files/Publications/WP/wp1711.ashx)). The book wound down through 2015–2017 as contracts expired and the BCB allowed maturities to roll off rather than rolling them forward.

**Effectiveness debate.** [Kohlscheen 2013 (BIS WP 426)](https://www.bis.org/publ/work426.htm) finds significant impact of BCB interventions using order-flow microstructure data, addressing the reverse-causality problem in earlier studies. [Chamon, Garcia and Souza 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022199617300521) (Journal of International Economics) use a synthetic-control approach and find that the August 2013 swap program produced a Real appreciation in excess of 10% relative to a synthetic counterfactual, with declining option-implied volatility; subsequent extensions had muted effects. Critics — including some [BIS Papers No. 104 contributions on EM FX intervention](https://www.bis.org/publ/bppdf/bispap104e.pdf) — argue effects were transitory and heavily dependent on Fed policy backdrop. Both Goldfajn-era and post-2016 BCB papers in the [Trabalhos para Discussão / Working Papers series](https://www.bcb.gov.br/pec/wps/ingl/wps509.pdf) (the Hedger of Last Resort paper, WP 509, is representative) lean toward "moderately effective at the margin, especially when used to address one-sided flow rather than to defend a level."

## 6. PTAX fixing

PTAX is the BCB's official BRL/USD reference rate and is **structurally distinct from the WMR 4pm London fix**. It is governed by [BCB Circular 3,506/2010](https://dadosabertos.bcb.gov.br/dataset/?tags=ptax) as updated by Circular 3,537/2011, which redesigned the methodology in response to manipulability concerns.

**Methodology.** Each business day the BCB conducts **four dealer polls**, at approximately 10:00, 11:00, 12:00, and 13:00 BRT. Within each window, the BCB picks a randomized two-minute sub-interval within a 10-minute capture range and asks accredited dealers (the BCB-published "Dealers" list) to provide bid and ask quotes. For each window, the **two highest and two lowest bids** are dropped, the **two highest and two lowest asks** are dropped, and the remaining quotes are averaged to produce a window bid-mid and a window ask-mid. The day's PTAX-bid is the simple mean of the four window bid-mids, and the day's PTAX-ask is the simple mean of the four window ask-mids; the BCB additionally publishes a derived PTAX-mid by applying a 0.0004 spread on either side of the midpoint ([CME Special Executive Report on PTAX](https://www.cmegroup.com/rulebook/files/S_5886.pdf); [BCB Open Data PTAX dataset](https://dadosabertos.bcb.gov.br/dataset/?tags=ptax)).

**Publication and contract usage.** The day's PTAX is finalized after the 13:00 window, typically published shortly after 13:15 BRT. The B3 DOL futures **expire on the first business day of the contract month and cash-settle to the PTAX of the immediately preceding business day** (so D-1 PTAX is the settlement print). CME 6L cash-settles to the BCB end-of-month PTAX of the last business day of the prior month (different convention). Many BRL-denominated swaps in the OTC and onshore market reference PTAX as their fixing source.

**Manipulation case.** In July 2015, the Brazilian competition authority **CADE** opened administrative proceeding 08700.004633/2015-04 against 15 global banks (Bank of Tokyo-Mitsubishi UFJ, Barclays, Citigroup, Credit Suisse, Deutsche Bank, HSBC, JP Morgan Chase, Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley, Nomura, RBC, RBS, Standard Chartered, UBS, and Banco Standard de Investimentos) plus 30 individuals, alleging cartel-style coordination on Bloomberg chats — internally referred to as "the cartel" or "the mafia" — to manipulate PTAX, WMR, and ECB reference rates from approximately 2007 to 2013 ([CADE press release on FX cartel](http://en.cade.gov.br/press-releases/cade-signs-five-agreements-regarding-a-cartel-investigation-in-the-foreign-exchange-market-and-opens-a-new-cartel-investigation-in-the-brazilian-exchange-market); [CADE earlier release](http://en.cade.gov.br/press-releases/cade2019s-superintendence-investigates-cartel-in-the-manipulation-of-exchange-rates)). On 7 December 2016, CADE signed five Cease-and-Desist Agreements (TCC) with five banks; pecuniary contributions totaled BRL 183.5 million. The investigation is part of the public record and is one motivation for the post-2011 PTAX methodology redesign with random sub-window timing.

## 7. Market hours and price discovery flow

**Asia → Europe → NY handover.** USD/BRL has thin Asia liquidity; BRL is the rare EM currency with no meaningful local Asian session footprint — discovery effectively starts when London opens. The principal liquidity windows are London open (≈04:00 BRT / 08:00 GMT) through the BCB intervention windows (10:00–13:00 BRT) into NY open (≈10:30 BRT / 09:30 ET). Onshore B3 DOL/WDO trade roughly 09:00–18:00 BRT, with extended hours on DOL/WDO until **18:30 BRT** since March 2024 ([B3 trading hours and extension announcements](https://globalexchanges.com/latest-news/brazil-b3-announces-extended-trading-hours/137682/); [B3 trading hours organized OTC page](https://www.b3.com.br/en_us/solutions/platforms/puma-trading-system/for-members-and-traders/trading-hours/organized-otc/)). Outside that window, price discovery is on CME 6L (Globex 17:00 CT Sunday through 16:00 CT Friday with the 16:00–17:00 CT daily break) and offshore NDF dealing rooms.

**US data releases.** Non-Farm Payrolls and CPI typically print at 08:30 ET (09:30 BRT), in advance of the BCB's first PTAX window (10:00 BRT); these systematically dislocate the cupom cambial curve and the casado, with the dislocation transmitted to the front-month DOL within minutes. FOMC decisions print at 14:00 ET (15:00 BRT), inside B3 hours, and tend to produce the largest single-print BRL moves of the year.

**Brazilian releases.** **IPCA** (the headline CPI measure) prints monthly around 09:00 BRT, before the first PTAX window. **COPOM** rate decisions land at the close of two-day meetings, typically Wednesday around 18:30 BRT, **after** the 18:00 BRT B3 close for spot but during the DOL extended session. The minutes (Ata) follow the next Tuesday at 08:00 BRT. The Quarterly Inflation/Monetary Policy Report follows roughly four weeks after each COPOM. The April 2026 COPOM cut Selic by 25bp to 14.50% and the bond and FX market reaction was driven primarily by the absence of forward guidance ([MercoPress, COPOM at 15% then 14.50%](https://en.mercopress.com/2025/12/11/brazil-selic-rate-kept-at-15-for-the-fourth-consecutive-time); [Rio Times Brazil inflation 2026 guide](https://www.riotimesonline.com/brazil-inflation-2026-guide/); [InvestingLive COPOM April 2026](https://investinglive.com/centralbank/brazils-cuts-rate-by-25bp-to-1450-but-flags-deanchored-inflation-and-middle-east-risks-20260429/)).

**BCB intervention windows.** When the BCB announces a swap or spot auction, it publishes the auction size and time on the [BCB Selic operations site](https://www3.bcb.gov.br/rasselic2023/leiloes/leiloes-de-swap-cambial); the auction itself typically completes within a 15-minute window. Liquidity ahead of and immediately after auctions is materially elevated; the casado and forward-points complex are the most active expression of the auction outcome.

## 8. Participant taxonomy

**Brazilian commercial banks.** Itaú Unibanco, Bradesco, Banco Santander Brasil, Banco do Brasil, and Caixa Econômica Federal collectively dominate corporate FX flow. Banco do Brasil specifically holds a disproportionate share of corporate FX (≈32.7% in older Greenwich rankings, [Coalition Greenwich Brazilian FX leaders](https://www.greenwich.com/press-release/leading-dealers-brokers-brazilian-fixed-income-fx-and-equities)) due to its agribusiness-export client base. Itaú is consistently the largest by interbank turnover.

**Foreign banks operating onshore.** JP Morgan, Citi, HSBC Brasil (now divested to Bradesco; HSBC retains some institutional presence), Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas, and Société Générale all maintain Brazilian banking subsidiaries authorized to operate FX (banco autorizado a operar em câmbio). They are the largest counterparties on the offshore-onshore basis (NDF vs DOL casado).

**Brazilian investment banks.** BTG Pactual is the largest local non-commercial bank and a significant FX market maker, particularly in derivatives; Safra is also active ([BTG Pactual câmbio empresarial](https://empresas.btgpactual.com/cambio)).

**Brazilian exporters and importers.** Vale (iron ore), Petrobras (oil), JBS and Marfrig (protein), and the agribusiness export complex (Cargill Brazil, Bunge, Cofco, ADM) are the largest USD inflow generators. Soybean exports are heavily seasonal: Brazilian soy ships principally **February through August** (peaking April–June after the main harvest), with corn (safrinha) shipping **August–December** ([Kpler Brazil soybean / freight](https://www.kpler.com/blog/brazil-looks-to-record-soybean-production-dry-freight-market-weakness-persists); [Breakwave on Brazil dry-bulk exports](https://www.breakwaveadvisors.com/insights/2023/8/12/brazil-dry-bulk-export-driver)). Iron ore shipments slow seasonally in Brazilian Q1 (rainy season interrupting Carajás operations). These flows show up directly in the BCB's published *fluxo cambial* (commercial vs financial flows) data.

**Real-money portfolio investors.** Foreign pension funds, sovereign-wealth funds, and EM mutual funds invest under 4,373 accounts; their flows are picked up in CVM and BCB statistics. Their FX hedging is split between onshore DOL and offshore NDF/CME 6L depending on operational setup.

**Foreign hedge funds.** Macro hedge funds expressing BRL views overwhelmingly do so via offshore NDF and CME 6L; only the largest (Brevan Howard, Bridgewater historically, Citadel, Millennium) maintain 4,373 accounts with onshore DOL access.

**Brazilian hedge funds and family offices.** A deep ecosystem (Verde Asset, SPX, Adam, Kapitalo, Legacy, Vinci, Itaú Asset, BB DTVM) trades DOL/WDO/DI extensively. Several local quant houses operate on B3 with co-located strategies.

**Retail.** B3 retail CPF accounts grew from 813,000 (December 2018) to over 5 million (2023) ([Brazil Stock Guide retail figures](https://brazilstockguide.com/insights/b3-2026-strategy-derivatives-retail-growth/)). Retail derivatives clients number roughly 250–300k actively trading minis; WDO is the dominant FX retail product. Retail ADTV on B3 is roughly BRL 3 billion.

**HFT and quant market makers.** Globally active firms (Susquehanna/SIG, Tower Research, Optiver, IMC, XTX, Citadel Securities, Jump) have varying degrees of presence in Brazil. Public confirmation of their B3 footprint is limited; primary-source description of HFT participation in B3 not located, secondary practitioner color suggests Tower and SIG have meaningful DOL/DI activity, and that B3's PUMA matching engine has hosted co-location since 2014. On CME 6L, the standard set of CME-resident HFT firms (Jump, Citadel, Hudson River) provides the bulk of liquidity.

## 9. Venue map

| Venue | Price-discovery weight | Typical participant mix | Session hours | Latency to public data | Hedging utility for offshore MM |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Onshore interbank spot (B3 FX Clearinghouse) | High during BRT business hours; sets the casado anchor | Brazilian commercial banks + foreign banks onshore via authorized dealers | 09:15–16:00 BRT | Near-zero from BCB feeds, ~5–10ms from B3 PUMA | Inaccessible to non-residents without 4,373 |
| Offshore NDF (London/NY) | Highest before 09:00 BRT and after 18:00 BRT; co-leads onshore during BRT hours | Foreign banks, real-money, hedge funds, EM crossover | 24h continuous OTC | Direct to Bloomberg/Refinitiv, ~50ms+ | Native venue for offshore book |
| CME 6L | Sets fair-value anchor when onshore is closed; secondary follower during BRT hours | CME HFT firms, hedge funds, asset managers; minimal Brazilian onshore participation | Sun 17:00 CT – Fri 16:00 CT, 16:00–17:00 CT daily halt | Direct CME data, sub-ms | Primary listed-derivative hedge for offshore book; basis to onshore via casado |
| B3 DOL | Co-leads price discovery during BRT hours; deepest BRL futures globally | Brazilian banks, hedge funds, foreign banks via subsidiaries, corporates | 09:00–18:00 BRT (DOL session); structured trades to 18:30 | B3 PUMA, ~10ms onshore | Inaccessible without 4,373 or onshore subsidiary |
| B3 WDO | Lags DOL on price; mirror contract; dominates retail flow | Retail, small institutions, hedge funds via 4,373 | 09:00–18:30 BRT | Same as DOL | Same access constraint |
| BCB swap / spot auctions | Dominant during announced auction windows | BCB vs accredited dealers | Auctions ~10:00–13:00 BRT typically | Auction results published immediately | None (offshore MM cannot bid); a pure shock channel |

The opinionated summary: **for an offshore market maker without a 4,373 vehicle, CME 6L is the only listed hedge available, and offshore NDF is the only continuous quoting venue**. Onshore basis (the casado plus the cupom cambial) is the residual risk that has to be modeled, not hedged. Real-time onshore price information is publicly available through B3 PUMA market data subscriptions and via secondary feeds (Bloomberg, Refinitiv); the 6L vs DOL basis is the offshore market maker's most observable proxy for BCB intervention imbalance.

## 10. Glossary of Portuguese FX terms

- **Casado** — "Married". The simultaneous spot-vs-futures USD trade that locks in the BRL-onshore-USD funding spread (cupom cambial); the spread itself is also called "the casado".
- **Cupom cambial** — The USD-denominated interest rate implied by Brazilian onshore arbitrage between BRL nominal rates and the USD/BRL forward curve. "Limpo" (clean, FRC) versus "sujo" (dirty, DDI).
- **Dólar futuro** — USD futures contract on B3, typically referring to DOL.
- **Leilão de linha** — USD repo auction by the BCB; sells USD to dealers with a future repurchase commitment.
- **Contrato a termo** — Forward contract; in Brazil, used for OTC USD/BRL forwards.
- **Swap cambial** — BCB-issued domestic non-deliverable forward (DNDF) settled in BRL against PTAX, where the BCB pays USD return and receives the cupom cambial coupon (or vice versa).
- **Swap reverso** — Reverse swap; BCB takes the long-USD side of the DNDF.
- **PTAX** — The BCB's official BRL/USD reference rate, computed via four daily dealer-poll windows.
- **Ponta vendida / ponta comprada** — "Short side / long side"; specifies which leg of a DNDF or swap a counterparty holds.
- **Posição de câmbio** — A bank's net FX position, recorded daily through SISBACEN. "Vendida em dólar" means short USD overall.
- **IOF** — Imposto sobre Operações Financeiras; the tax on financial operations, including a specific IOF-FX category.
- **ROF (Registro de Operação Financeira)** — BCB registration required for inbound foreign loans and certain cross-border financings.
- **Corretora** — Brokerage; an authorized intermediary distinct from a banco.
- **Distribuidora** — Securities distributor; another non-bank intermediary class.
- **Mesa de câmbio** — FX desk inside a bank.
- **Banco autorizado a operar em câmbio** — Bank with BCB authorization to operate in FX.
- **Fluxo cambial** — Published BCB statistic of net commercial vs financial cross-border BRL flows.
- **Balança comercial** — Trade balance; published monthly by the Ministry of Development, Industry and Foreign Trade (MDIC) and feeds the commercial component of fluxo cambial.

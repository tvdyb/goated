# Phase 02 — FX Market Making Pricing Models (General → EM-Specific)

This document is the canonical pricing-model reference for the 16-phase USD/BRL market-making research program. It surveys the academic and practitioner literature on dealer pricing, bid-ask spread formation, inventory management, and adverse-selection skewing, then narrows to the frictions that actually bind in EM and specifically in Brazilian-real markets. Phase 1 (`research/phase_01_fx_market_structure.md`) is assumed as common ground: the reader is expected to know the venues, the PTAX fixing methodology, the BCB intervention instruments, the casado, and the participant taxonomy that this document will repeatedly refer back to.

The internal logic of the document is: build the canonical theory in roughly chronological order (Glosten-Milgrom → Kyle → PIN/VPIN → Lyons → Avellaneda-Stoikov → Guéant-Lehalle-Tapia → Cartea-Jaimungal); place it inside the dealer-vs-execution distinction (Section 6); then layer the EM-specific frictions and the practitioner mechanics on top (Sections 7–8); finally synthesize what an EM-FX market maker actually solves (Section 9) and identify which USD/BRL participants are presumptively informed (Section 10). The closing note explains how this material feeds Phase 14, where the actual quote policy will be built.

Notation is fixed as follows throughout: $S_t$ is the (mid) price of the asset at time $t$; $V$ the unobserved fundamental value; $\sigma$ the diffusion volatility; $q$ (or $Q$) the dealer's signed inventory; $\gamma$ the dealer's CARA risk-aversion parameter; $T$ the terminal horizon; $\delta^a$ and $\delta^b$ the dealer's ask and bid offsets from mid; $k$ the exponential decay parameter of the order-arrival intensity; $\Lambda(\delta) = A e^{-k\delta}$ the order-arrival intensity at distance $\delta$; $\lambda$ Kyle's price-impact coefficient. Where a different paper uses different symbols, the most common usage in the recent literature is preferred and the original notation is given in parentheses on first use.

## 1. Glosten and Milgrom (1985): adverse selection and the pure-information spread

The Glosten-Milgrom (henceforth GM) sequential-trade model, published in the *Journal of Financial Economics*, is the canonical adverse-selection foundation for dealer quoting ([Glosten and Milgrom 1985, JFE](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0304405X85900443); [Stanford working version](https://milgrom.people.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/1984/09/Bid-Ask-and-Transaction-Prices.pdf)). Trade arrives one unit at a time. Each arriving trader is informed with probability $\alpha$ and uninformed with probability $1-\alpha$. Informed traders know the realization of a binary terminal value $V \in \{V_L, V_H\}$ with prior probability of the high state $\theta = \Pr[V = V_H]$. Uninformed traders buy or sell with equal probability, modeling liquidity demand. The risk-neutral, competitive market maker observes only the side of each arriving order and is forced by Bertrand competition to set bid and ask quotes equal to her conditional expectation of $V$ given that an arriving order is, respectively, a sell or a buy. The bid and ask are therefore

$$ a_t = \mathbb{E}[V \mid \text{buy at } t] = \frac{\theta(1+\alpha)V_H + (1-\theta)(1-\alpha)V_L}{\theta(1+\alpha) + (1-\theta)(1-\alpha)} $$

$$ b_t = \mathbb{E}[V \mid \text{sell at } t] = \frac{\theta(1-\alpha)V_H + (1-\theta)(1+\alpha)V_L}{\theta(1-\alpha) + (1-\theta)(1+\alpha)} $$

The spread $a_t - b_t > 0$ exists even though the dealer is risk-neutral and earns zero expected profit per trade: it is pure compensation for the conditional probability that the next arriving counterparty knows more than the dealer does. The size of the spread scales monotonically in $\alpha$ (the probability of an informed counterparty) and in the dispersion $V_H - V_L$ (the magnitude of the adverse-selection problem). Quotes are martingales — each transaction triggers a Bayesian update of $\theta$ and therefore of subsequent quotes — and after enough trades private information is fully impounded into prices ([Chamley lecture notes](https://people.bu.edu/chamley/0-EC-717/notes7(GM).pdf)).

The model's relevance to FX dealing is structural rather than mechanical. FX dealers do not see a one-shot binary fundamental, but they do face exactly the adverse-selection problem GM formalizes: every incoming RFQ or hit on a stream is potentially from a counterparty with material non-public information (a pending corporate flow, an upcoming central-bank auction signal, a stop-loss level seen elsewhere, an algorithmic execution that will be persistent). The minimum width of the dealer's spread on a given client tier is the GM spread implied by that tier's $\alpha$. This is the formal underpinning of the practitioner observation that disclosed-relationship clients receive tighter quotes than anonymous flow on EBS or Refinitiv Matching: the dealer's $\alpha$ for a known corporate hedger is structurally lower than for unknown anonymized flow.

GM also gives the modeler a cleaner separation between two distinct components of the spread that show up combined in practice: (i) the **adverse-selection component**, equal to the post-trade revision in the dealer's expectation; and (ii) the **inventory-holding component** (developed in the Stoll-Ho-Avellaneda-Stoikov line in Sections 5 below), which is compensation for warehousing risk rather than for information ([Glosten-Harris 1988](https://www.acsu.buffalo.edu/~keechung/MGF743/Readings/B3%20Glosten%20and%20Harris,%201988%20JFE.pdf)). The decomposition matters in EM because the two components have very different EM-specific magnitudes: adverse-selection is structurally larger in BRL than in EUR (because local participants have informational advantages relative to the consolidated tape that an offshore dealer can see), while inventory cost can be smaller for a hedged book that turns inventory in CME 6L within minutes.

## 2. Kyle (1985, 1989): price impact and the depth coefficient λ

Kyle's "Continuous Auctions and Insider Trading" (*Econometrica* 1985) is the second foundational pillar ([Kyle 1985](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1913210); [hosted full-text PDF](https://people.duke.edu/~qc2/BA532/1985%20EMA%20Kyle.pdf)). Three classes of agents trade a single risky asset over a finite horizon: a single risk-neutral insider knows the terminal value $v \sim \mathcal{N}(p_0, \Sigma_0)$; noise traders submit aggregate net order flow $u_t$ with $u_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_u^2 \Delta t)$; and a competitive risk-neutral market maker observes only the aggregate order flow $y_t = x_t + u_t$ (insider order $x_t$ plus noise $u_t$) and sets a price equal to her conditional expectation of $v$ given the history of $y$.

In the single-period version, the insider chooses $x = \beta(v - p_0)$ and the market maker sets $p = p_0 + \lambda y$, yielding the celebrated equilibrium formulas

$$ \beta = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma_u^2}{\Sigma_0}}, \qquad \lambda = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\Sigma_0}{\sigma_u^2}}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[\text{insider profit}] = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\Sigma_0 \sigma_u^2} $$

so that $\lambda$ — the **price-impact coefficient**, "Kyle's lambda" — is half the ratio of the standard deviation of fundamental value to the standard deviation of noise-trade flow. In the continuous-time limit the market is constant-depth: $\lambda$ does not move over the trading session; price follows a Brownian motion under the market maker's filtration; and all of the insider's information is revealed by the terminal date ([Kyle's lambda explainer, frds](https://frds.io/measures/kyle_lambda/)). Kyle (1989, *Review of Economic Studies*) extends to multiple imperfectly competitive informed traders, with the result that as the number of insiders increases price discovery accelerates and depth (the inverse of $\lambda$) increases.

The translation to FX dealing is that $\lambda$ is the linear coefficient mapping order-flow imbalance to subsequent mid-price moves, and it is empirically the most-fitted single quantity in FX microstructure. Evans and Lyons' regression (Section 4) of $\Delta s_t$ on signed interdealer order flow has the form $\Delta s_t = \alpha + \lambda \cdot \text{OF}_t + \epsilon_t$ with positive, statistically robust $\lambda$. For a dealer the operational consequence is that the inventory she takes on now will move the mid against her by $\lambda \cdot q$ before she can hedge it, so an inventory-aware quote must skew by approximately $\lambda \cdot q$ toward unwinding the position. Estimation of $\lambda$ in EM-FX is the empirical content of the Kohlscheen (2013) BIS BRL paper discussed in Section 7 ([Kohlscheen 2013, BIS WP 426](https://www.bis.org/publ/work426.htm)), where intraday customer-flow data give a directly measurable $\lambda$ on USD/BRL.

Kyle and GM solve different problems but are formally compatible: GM yields a static inside-the-spread no-arbitrage condition for the dealer's bid and ask given a single arriving counterparty; Kyle gives the asymptotic linear pricing rule when the dealer aggregates many small orders. A practical FX dealer uses GM to widen spreads on tiers with high $\alpha$ and uses Kyle's $\lambda$ to skew the mid (or equivalently, the reservation price) in the direction that offsets her current inventory.

## 3. PIN and VPIN: empirical adverse-selection in trade data

The Easley, Kiefer, O'Hara, Paperman (EKOP, 1996) **PIN** (Probability of Informed Trading) model operationalizes GM ([Easley-Kiefer-O'Hara-Paperman 1996, JF](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1540-6261.1996.tb04074.x)). On each trading day, an information event occurs with probability $\alpha$; if it occurs, it is bad news with probability $\delta$ and good news with probability $1-\delta$. Informed traders arrive only on the side consistent with the realized signal at Poisson rate $\mu$. Uninformed buys arrive at rate $\epsilon_b$ and uninformed sells at rate $\epsilon_s$ regardless of state. The likelihood of the day's observed signed-trade counts $(B, S)$ is a mixture of three Poisson-pair densities, and PIN is the structural estimate of the probability that any given trade is information-motivated:

$$ \text{PIN} = \frac{\alpha \mu}{\alpha \mu + \epsilon_b + \epsilon_s} $$

Estimation requires only signed trade counts (which Lee-Ready or tick-rule classifications can produce from public TRADE data) and a numerical maximization of the multinomial likelihood over the four parameters $(\alpha, \delta, \mu, \epsilon)$. PIN has been extended to multilayer models (more than one information event per day), Bayesian estimators that handle the boundary cases, and adjusted-PIN variants that mitigate misspecification under symmetric order-flow shocks ([PINstimation R package](https://journal.r-project.org/articles/RJ-2023-044/)).

PIN's FX applications are thinner than its equity applications because tick-by-tick FX data with reliable trade signing is harder to obtain than equity TAQ. The most cited FX-adjacent application is the [ECB Working Paper 987](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/pub/pdf/scpwps/ecbwp987.pdf), which estimates PIN on the euro overnight money market and finds that intraday PIN moves systematically around ECB liquidity-providing operations. For EM currencies, PIN estimation on dealer-customer data has been used in the Brazilian context but the disaggregated data underlying Kohlscheen (2013) are not public, so direct PIN-on-USDBRL estimates are not reproducible from open data. The conceptual takeaway for an EM-FX market maker is that PIN provides the right structural decomposition: the dealer's spread should respond to *time-varying* PIN, not to a static $\alpha$, and PIN spikes around scheduled events (USDA WASDE in commodities, COPOM and IPCA in BRL, FOMC across all currencies).

The **VPIN** (Volume-synchronized PIN) extension of Easley, López de Prado, and O'Hara (2011, 2012) replaces calendar-time bucketing with volume-time bucketing: instead of estimating PIN over fixed-clock intervals, the data are sorted into equal-volume buckets and the order-imbalance fraction is computed within each bucket ([Easley-López de Prado-O'Hara, "Flow Toxicity"](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1748633); [VPIN improved version](https://cfr.ivo-welch.info/published/papers/easley-prado-hara.pdf)). Formally, with bucket size $V$ and within-bucket buy and sell volumes $V_b^\tau$ and $V_s^\tau$,

$$ \text{VPIN}_\tau = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=\tau-n+1}^{\tau} \frac{|V_b^j - V_s^j|}{V} $$

VPIN is a real-time toxicity barometer: a sustained high reading signals that liquidity providers are systematically taking the wrong side of an imbalance and is empirically associated with the May 2010 Flash Crash and other liquidity dislocations. Its FX-relevant interpretation is that the dealer can compute VPIN on her own fill book in close to real time, and use elevated VPIN as a trigger to widen spreads or pull quotes — i.e., a policy lever at exactly the timescale a 30-second quoting loop operates on.

The principal critique — see [Andersen and Bondarenko, "VPIN and the flash crash" (JFM 2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1386418113000189) — is that VPIN's predictive power is sensitive to bucket-size choice and to the trade-classification rule. For an EM-FX dealer this matters because trade signs in OTC FX are not directly observable from a public tape; a dealer must use her own fill data, and the resulting VPIN is conditioned on her own client tier rather than a market aggregate. The signal is still useful but should be interpreted as "toxicity in *my* flow" rather than "market-wide toxicity," which is in fact the more relevant quantity for the dealer's own risk.

## 4. The Lyons FX microstructure approach

Richard Lyons' research program — collected in [*The Microstructure Approach to Exchange Rates* (MIT Press 2001)](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262622059/the-microstructure-approach-to-exchange-rates/) and developed across a decade of papers with Martin Evans — is the FX-specific reformulation of the adverse-selection-and-order-flow microstructure paradigm. The empirical anchor is that traditional macro fundamentals (interest-rate differentials, money supplies, current-account balances) explain at most 10% of the day-to-day variation in major-currency exchange rates, while interdealer order flow alone explains 40–80%. The most-cited expression of this is [Evans and Lyons (2002), *Journal of Political Economy*](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/324391): a parsimonious model of daily DEM/USD changes with interdealer order flow as a regressor produces an in-sample $R^2$ above 60%, and a $1 billion net dollar purchase moves DEM/USD by about 0.5%.

The Lyons synthesis rests on three mechanisms. First, **information aggregation**: end-user (corporate, hedge fund, real-money, central bank) trades are dispersed and not seen by all dealers; their information content reaches the consolidated mid only after dealers have traded with each other and pushed signed flow into the interdealer market, where it is finally observable. Second, **hot-potato trading**: when a dealer takes on inventory she does not want, she trades it out with another dealer, who then trades it out with another, and so on; this multiplies interdealer turnover relative to the underlying customer flow by an order of magnitude and explains why interdealer turnover has historically been many multiples of dealer-customer turnover. Third, **order-flow-as-private-information**: order flow forecasts not just future exchange rates but future macro fundamentals themselves — output, inflation, money growth — because end-user trading reveals the firms' and asset managers' private information about those fundamentals before public macro statistics catch up ([Evans and Lyons 2007/2012, *Quarterly Journal of Finance*](https://www.nber.org/papers/w13151)).

The implications for an FX market maker are direct. First, the dealer's mid should be conditioned not just on the public reference (Reuters/Bloomberg or a CLS-derived synthetic) but on a signed order-flow estimate; in practice this enters as a Kyle-type linear adjustment with $\lambda$ estimated rolling, and the EM equivalent is exactly what Kohlscheen (2013) does for BRL. Second, the dealer should distinguish between client types because their flow has different information content: corporate exporter flow is largely uninformed (a Vale or JBS hedge is a calendar-driven cashflow), whereas hedge-fund macro flow is presumptively informed. Third, the hot-potato effect means that observed interdealer flow over-states underlying information: the same end-user dollar gets passed around the dealer network and shows up in interdealer flow several times, so an order-flow regression that fails to filter for inter-dealer pass-through will overstate $\lambda$. The fix in modern practice is to use customer flow when available (as in Brandt-Kavajecz on Treasuries; [Brandt and Kavajecz 2004](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=336620)) or to use prime-broker-disclosed end-user flow.

The corroborating dealer-level evidence is in [Bjønnes and Rime (2005, *Journal of Financial Economics*)](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v75y2005i3p571-605.html), which examines four interbank dealers' complete trade records over a week. They find strong inventory-control behavior (consistent with Section 5 below) but, importantly, that the dealers control inventory primarily by hitting other dealers' quotes on electronic brokers rather than by skewing their own quotes — a point that maps directly onto the modern FX dealer who internalizes some flow and externalizes the rest (Section 8). They also find that the information content of incoming trades is concentrated in the *direction* of the trade rather than in its *size*, which is a useful empirical input for sizing the GM-style adverse-selection component.

A complementary recent contribution is [Mancini, Ranaldo, and Wrampelmeyer (2013, *Journal of Finance*)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jofi.12053), which provides the first systematic measurement of FX-market liquidity and finds substantial commonality across currencies and significant carry-style risk premia for FX-illiquidity exposure. Importantly, their findings show that liquidity commonality is weaker in EM (R² around 19%) than in developed pairs (R² around 38%), implying that an EM-FX dealer cannot rely on a single global FX-liquidity factor as a sufficient summary of her own liquidity environment — she must condition on EM-specific drivers.

## 5. Continuous-time inventory models: Avellaneda-Stoikov, Guéant-Lehalle-Tapia, Cartea-Jaimungal

The continuous-time inventory-control class is the workhorse of modern algorithmic market making. Its lineage traces back to Garman (1976) — the first formal microstructure model, in which a monopolistic dealer faces Poisson arrivals on each side and must avoid the "ruin problem" of a sided imbalance ([Garman 1976, JFE](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0304405X76900064)) — and through Stoll (1978), which adds explicit inventory-holding cost ([Stoll 1978](https://www.kansascityfed.org/documents/5425/pdf-RWP00-03.pdf)), and Ho-Stoll (1981), which solves the dealer's expected-utility maximization via stochastic dynamic programming and produces inventory-skewed bid and ask quotes ([Ho-Stoll 1981, JFE](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0304405X81900209)).

The modern reformulation is **Avellaneda and Stoikov (2008)** ([Avellaneda-Stoikov 2008, *Quantitative Finance*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14697680701381228); [hosted PDF](https://people.orie.cornell.edu/sfs33/LimitOrderBook.pdf)). The model: the mid price evolves as a Brownian motion with volatility $\sigma$, $dS_t = \sigma \, dW_t$. The dealer posts a bid at $S_t - \delta^b$ and an ask at $S_t + \delta^a$. Buy market orders arrive at the dealer's ask at intensity $\Lambda^a(\delta^a) = A e^{-k \delta^a}$ and sell market orders at the bid at intensity $\Lambda^b(\delta^b) = A e^{-k \delta^b}$, with $A$ and $k$ constants estimated from historical fills. The dealer has CARA utility with risk aversion $\gamma$ and terminal horizon $T$. The HJB equation for the dealer's value function $u(s, q, t)$ admits an asymptotic expansion in two steps. Step 1: compute the **reservation (indifference) price**

$$ r(s, q, t) = s - q\,\gamma\,\sigma^2\,(T-t) $$

which is the price at which the dealer is indifferent between her current inventory and a zero-inventory baseline. Step 2: place quotes symmetrically around the reservation price, with total spread

$$ \delta^a + \delta^b = \gamma\,\sigma^2\,(T-t) + \frac{2}{\gamma}\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{\gamma}{k}\right) $$

The optimal individual offsets are

$$ \delta^a = \frac{\delta^a + \delta^b}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\,q\,\gamma\,\sigma^2\,(T-t), \quad \delta^b = \frac{\delta^a + \delta^b}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\,q\,\gamma\,\sigma^2\,(T-t) $$

so the spread depends only on volatility, time-to-horizon, and the arrival/risk-aversion ratio, while the **skew** $r - s = -q \gamma \sigma^2 (T-t)$ depends on inventory. Notation: $s$ is the current mid, $q$ is signed inventory in shares (long positive), $\sigma^2 (T-t)$ is the residual variance to horizon, $\gamma$ is CARA risk aversion (units 1/[currency]), $k$ measures order-book sensitivity to the dealer's quoting distance (units 1/[price]), and $A$ is a baseline arrival rate.

Comparison to the naive symmetric strategy in simulation shows that the Avellaneda-Stoikov (henceforth **AS**) strategy yields a P&L distribution with much lower variance and a similar mean, the central robustness result of the paper. Two limitations are immediately relevant for FX: (i) the model assumes symmetric arrival rates $A$ on bid and ask, which is empirically violated whenever there is a directional flow imbalance; and (ii) the Brownian midprice rules out jumps, which is a strong assumption in EM where overnight gaps and intervention shocks are common. Both are addressed in subsequent extensions.

**Guéant, Lehalle, and Fernandez-Tapia (2013)**, in *Mathematics and Financial Economics*, provide the rigorous closed-form solution for the AS problem under inventory constraints by transforming the HJB system into a system of linear ODEs ([GLT 2013, MFE](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11579-012-0087-0); [arXiv version](https://arxiv.org/abs/1105.3115)). They show that under bounded inventory $|q| \le Q_{\max}$, the optimal quotes are essentially the AS quotes capped at the boundary, and the closed-form structure makes the policy easy to implement at run-time. Their formulation is the one most production market-making engines actually solve: it gives a finite-state Markov decision process with inventory as the state and a small number of free parameters $(\sigma, k, A, \gamma, Q_{\max})$ that must be estimated offline.

**Cartea, Jaimungal, and Penalva (2015)**, in their textbook [*Algorithmic and High-Frequency Trading* (Cambridge University Press)](https://assets.cambridge.org/97811070/91146/frontmatter/9781107091146_frontmatter.pdf), generalize this further to include adverse selection (the mid drifts in the direction of the dealer's fill at rate proportional to fill size), latency, and market impact from the dealer's own hedging trades. Their development of **mutually-exciting (Hawkes) order arrivals** ([Cartea-Jaimungal-Ricci, *SIAM Review*](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3306158)) lets the dealer condition arrival intensities on recent flow rather than treating them as constant — the analytical analogue of the practitioner intuition that "flow begets flow." For FX the most operationally important Cartea-Jaimungal generalization is the multi-asset version where the dealer holds inventory in correlated assets and quotes a single book; this maps directly onto the offshore USD/BRL market maker quoting NDF and hedging with CME 6L, where the two are correlated but not identical.

The **FX-specific** extensions are concentrated in a recent literature anchored by [Barzykin and Guéant (2023, *Mathematical Finance*)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/mafi.12367) and [Barzykin, Bergault, Guéant (2021, "Market making by an FX dealer," *arXiv* 2112.02269)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.02269). They embed three FX-specific features into the AS-GLT framework: (i) **client tiers** with different price-elasticity parameters $k_i$ — small clients have low $k$ (price-insensitive, can be charged a wide spread) and large macro funds have high $k$ (price-sensitive, must be quoted tight); (ii) **pricing ladders** where the dealer's quote varies in size as well as in price; and (iii) **hedging-rate optimization** where the dealer chooses how aggressively to externalize inventory in the interbank market versus warehouse it. The empirical work in their paper shows that two distinct client clusters (tiers) emerge naturally from G10 dealer-client trade data, justifying the dealer's standard practice of pricing-ladder differentiation.

The continuous-time inventory class is the right starting point for an EM-FX market maker but it is incomplete: it assumes a Brownian midprice (no jumps), constant arrival rates (no information asymmetry), and a representative client (no tiering). The Barzykin-Guéant paper repairs the third gap; Cartea-Jaimungal repair the first; and the second requires merging in the GM/PIN/VPIN machinery from Sections 1–3. The merged object is the structure of the quoting model that Phase 14 will actually build.

## 6. Optimal-execution theory adjacent to MM

The execution-side literature solves a different problem from market making: given a *fixed* quantity to trade by a *fixed* deadline, what schedule minimizes the expected cost of trading (or the mean-variance combination of cost and timing risk)? The dealer cares about this only insofar as her hedging leg must execute her residual inventory in CME 6L or in onshore DOL (when she has access). Three references cover the field.

[Bertsimas and Lo (1998, *Journal of Financial Markets*)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=7395) frame execution as a discrete-time stochastic dynamic program: minimize expected cost of executing a block of $X$ shares in $T$ periods given a linear price-impact function $p_t = p_{t-1} + \theta x_t + u_t$ with i.i.d. mean-zero innovation $u_t$. The closed-form solution under linear impact and no risk aversion is *equal-share execution* — the trivial TWAP — and the paper's contribution is the extension to information-conditioned price-impact functions where the optimal schedule is no longer flat.

[Almgren and Chriss (1999, "Optimal Execution of Portfolio Transactions")](https://www.smallake.kr/wp-content/uploads/2016/03/optliq.pdf) add a mean-variance objective: minimize $\mathbb{E}[\text{cost}] + \lambda_{\text{trader}} \cdot \text{Var}[\text{cost}]$ where $\lambda_{\text{trader}}$ is the trader's risk aversion. The model splits price impact into permanent and temporary components: a permanent component $g(v)$ that persists as a function of trading rate $v$, and a temporary component $h(v)$ that affects only the current execution price. Under linear $g$ and $h$ and Brownian midprice, the optimal trajectory is a deterministic exponential decay of the unfilled inventory at rate $\kappa = \sqrt{\lambda \sigma^2 / \eta}$ (where $\eta$ is the temporary-impact slope and $\sigma$ is the price volatility): more risk-averse traders front-load (large $\kappa$); less risk-averse traders are willing to spread the trade out further. The Almgren-Chriss "efficient frontier" of execution strategies is the most-cited construct in the implementation-shortfall industry, and an enormous practitioner literature has built on it ([Anboto Labs deep-dive](https://medium.com/@anboto_labs/deep-dive-into-is-the-almgren-chriss-framework-be45a1bde831)).

[Obizhaeva and Wang (2013, *Journal of Financial Markets*)](https://web.mit.edu/wangj/www/pap/ObizhaevaWang13.pdf) repair the most uncomfortable Almgren-Chriss assumption — that the limit-order-book impact function is static — by introducing **resilience**: the order book recovers from a deviation at exponential rate $\rho$. Under this dynamic, the optimal strategy is no longer a smooth trajectory: it begins with a discrete trade that knocks the book down, then continuously trades at a rate matching the resilience, and ends with another discrete trade. Resilience-aware execution is operationally important for an offshore EM-FX dealer because CME 6L has thinner depth than EUR/USD, so the resilience parameter $\rho$ is smaller and the optimal hedge schedule has more discrete-trade structure than the smooth Almgren-Chriss intuition suggests.

The bridge from execution theory back to market making is via the dealer's **residual inventory hedging policy**: given that the dealer ends each interval with a non-zero $q$, the cost of unwinding $q$ over the next interval is exactly an Almgren-Chriss problem with the dealer's own inventory as the block size. The AS-GLT $\gamma \sigma^2 (T-t)$ skew implicitly assumes that inventory will be held to horizon; if the dealer instead hedges actively, the effective $\gamma$ in the AS framework is depressed by the hedge cost-of-carry, and the shape of the optimal quoting policy compresses. Barzykin-Bergault-Guéant's hedging-rate parameter is the explicit formal handle on this trade-off.

## 7. EM-specific frictions: where the standard machinery breaks

The frameworks above (Glosten-Milgrom, Kyle, Avellaneda-Stoikov, Almgren-Chriss) were developed and validated principally on US equity, US Treasury, or G10 FX data, where the assumptions of stationary diffusion, symmetric information access, light intervention, and continuous trading are at least approximately correct. EM and especially BRL violate every one of these, and the violations are the dominant source of P&L for an EM-FX market maker.

**Wider spreads.** The starting fact is that EM FX bid-ask spreads in calm regimes are 5–10× G10 norms in stress, and even in calm 2–4× wider on top-of-book ([BIS WP 426, Kohlscheen 2013](https://www.bis.org/publ/work426.htm); [BIS Quarterly Review on EM FX intervention, Domanski, Kohlscheen, Moreno 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1609f.htm)). Two structural reasons: capital-controls-driven segmentation (offshore NDFs and onshore DOL trade in disconnected pools, and the casado that links them moves with capital-control regime), and informational asymmetry — local dealers see the full SISBACEN-registered FX flow daily, while offshore dealers see only the slice their own clients show them.

**Asymmetric information from local banks.** The BIS literature on EM FX dealing repeatedly emphasizes that local banks have an informational edge ([BIS Quarterly Review, December 2019, Schrimpf-Sushko on FX trade execution](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1912g.pdf); [BIS WP 1094, Chaboud-Rime-Sushko, "The Foreign Exchange Market," 2023](https://www.bis.org/publ/work1094.pdf)). In Brazil this is institutionalized: the BCB's accredited Dealer list gives a small group of local banks privileged access to PTAX-poll information and to first-round notification of swap auctions. Itaú, Bradesco, Santander Brasil, and Banco do Brasil collectively see flow that no offshore market participant can replicate. For an offshore market maker the practical translation is that the GM probability $\alpha$ on the offshore side of the casado is structurally elevated — if your counterparty is a São Paulo-based dealer who saw the BCB auction-pre-announcement at SISBACEN, $\alpha$ on that fill is close to one.

**Intervention regimes that break stationarity.** Phase 1 documented the BCB intervention complex: spot auctions, line auctions, traditional swaps (DNDFs), reverse swaps, and cupom cambial intervention. Each is a discontinuous shock to the price process that violates the AS Brownian-mid assumption and the Kyle constant-depth result. The empirical literature on intervention effectiveness — [Kohlscheen (2013, BIS WP 426)](https://www.bis.org/publ/work426.htm); [Chamon, Garcia, Souza (2017, *Journal of International Economics*)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022199617300521); [Garcia and Volpon, "DNDFs"](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/DNDFs:a-more-efficient-way-to-intervene-in-FX-Garcia-Volpon/8d2c0e57e53b87f83d8ba8a6f837625ce34a3275/figure/1); [BIS WP 499, Chamon and Garcia, "Foreign exchange intervention strategies and effectiveness"](https://www.bis.org/publ/work499.pdf) — finds significant intervention impact, particularly via the August-2013 BCB swap program where Chamon-Garcia-Souza estimate a >10% appreciation versus a synthetic counterfactual. The practical consequence for the dealer is that intervention windows must be *gated*: the AS quoting policy must be paused or radically widened around announced auctions, because the price process inside the auction window is not the same Brownian motion the dealer calibrated $\sigma$ against. The model's volatility parameter is conditional on the regime, and switching between calm and intervention regimes requires regime detection (a hidden-Markov layer over the AS skeleton) rather than a single $\sigma$.

**Jump risk around political news.** EM FX shows fat tails relative to G10 because of frequent regime-shifting political shocks: a single political-crisis day in Brasília can move USD/BRL 3–5% in seconds. The AS framework is silent on jumps; Cartea-Jaimungal's textbook adds Poisson jump components to the midprice but the parameter that matters for the dealer — the jump intensity — is non-stationary and event-driven. The operationally relevant fix is not to model the jumps but to *gate* on the calendar: the AS skew is paused during pre-defined event windows (COPOM, IPCA, FOMC, US payrolls, scheduled BCB auctions, presidential addresses with known content). This is the EM analogue of the equity HFT gate around earnings releases ([Cartea-Jaimungal-Penalva 2015 textbook](https://assets.cambridge.org/97811070/91146/frontmatter/9781107091146_frontmatter.pdf), chapter on event-driven trading).

**Regime switches between calm and crisis.** The MRW (Mancini-Ranaldo-Wrampelmeyer) result that EM FX has weaker liquidity commonality than G10 implies that an EM-FX dealer cannot rely on a global FX-liquidity index to forecast her own regime; she must condition on local indicators (BCB swap-book size, casado spread, offshore-onshore basis, fluxo cambial). The standard summary is the Mancini-Ranaldo-Wrampelmeyer FX-liquidity factor specialized to EM, which has a clear two-state structure (calm vs. stressed) detectable in real time. The regime-switching layer over the AS skeleton is the practical implementation: in the calm state, AS quoting with EM-calibrated $\sigma$; in the stressed state, the dealer pulls quotes or widens substantially.

The combined message is that the AS-GLT-CJ machinery is the *core* of an EM-FX market maker but the binding constraints are at the boundary: regime detection (calm vs. stress), event-window gating (intervention, USDA/COPOM/FOMC), and tier-conditioned $\alpha$ estimation. The principal published synthesis of EM intervention practice — [Domanski, Kohlscheen, and Moreno (2016, BIS Quarterly Review)](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1609f.htm) and the broader [BIS Papers No. 104 on EM FX intervention](https://www.bis.org/publ/bppdf/bispap104e.pdf) — should be read as a regime catalogue: the dealer's AS calibration must change across the regimes those papers describe.

## 8. Dealer-quote practitioner mechanics in FX

The academic models above translate into a fairly stable set of practitioner mechanics in FX. The synthesis here is drawn principally from [Schrimpf-Sushko (BIS Quarterly Review, December 2019, "FX trade execution: complex and highly fragmented")](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1912g.pdf), [Chaboud-Rime-Sushko (BIS WP 1094, 2023, "The Foreign Exchange Market")](https://www.bis.org/publ/work1094.pdf), [Drehmann-Sushko (BIS QR December 2022, "Global FX in higher-volatility environment")](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2212f.pdf), the [GFXC FX Global Code](https://www.globalfxc.org/uploads/fx_global.pdf), and the [Butz-Oomen "Internalisation by electronic FX spot dealers" (*Quantitative Finance* 2019)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14697688.2018.1504167) paper.

**Skew on spot, vol, and risk reversals.** A dealer facing a long-USD inventory wants to encourage USD-buying flow and discourage USD-selling flow. She does this by skewing her quote: lowering her ask (cheaper to buy USD from her) and lowering her bid (less attractive to sell USD to her). The amount of skew is exactly the AS reservation-price shift $-q \gamma \sigma^2 (T-t)$. On the **vol** dimension, the same logic applies: a dealer net-short vega skews her vol quote down to attract vol-buying flow. On **risk-reversal** (RR — the difference in implied vol between equidistant OTM call and OTM put), EM dealers show a particularly strong positive RR tilt because of one-way demand for downside protection on the EM currency; an EM-FX dealer net-long downside (i.e., having sold puts on the local currency in size) skews her RR quote toward call-richness to attract offsetting flow. The general FX vol-quote convention — ATM, 25-delta RR, 25-delta butterfly — and its delta and ATM-strike conventions are summarized in standard practitioner references ([Reiswich and Wystup, "FX volatility smile construction"](https://www.academia.edu/29581458/FX_volatility_smile_construction); [Quantpie FX RR/Strangle](https://www.quantpie.co.uk/fx/fx_rr_str.php)).

**Size tiers.** A modern FX dealer maintains multiple price ladders for different counterparties. Empirically and in the formal model of [Barzykin-Bergault-Guéant (2021)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.02269), client elasticity $k$ in the AS arrival function is bimodal: small/transactional clients (corporate hedgers, retail brokers) have low $k$ — they will execute even at wide spreads — while large macro clients (hedge funds, real-money asset managers) have high $k$ — they will route to a competitor if the dealer's price is not on top. The dealer's optimal policy is to charge wider spreads to low-$k$ clients (where the dealer extracts more economic rent per fill) and tighter spreads to high-$k$ clients (where the dealer prioritizes fill rate to keep relationship volume). Operationally this is the "price-ladder" feature of every modern dealer's quote engine: a single mid drives multiple bid-ask pairs, one per client tier, each with its own width and skew.

**Quote fade and last look.** Last look is the dealer's option to reject an incoming trade request after she has streamed a quote. It originated in the late-1990s electronic FX market to protect the dealer from stale-data hits during fast moves and from latency arbitrage by faster counterparties ([Flextrade, "A Hard Look at Last Look"](https://flextrade.com/resources/a-hard-look-at-last-look-in-foreign-exchange/)). In modern practice it is still widespread on dealer-streamed venues — single-dealer platforms, MDPs like FXall and Hotspot, certain anonymized streams — though the GFXC's Principle 17 in the FX Global Code ([GFXC last-look guidance](https://www.globalfxc.org/uploads/gfxc_report_last_look-1.pdf)) requires dealers to disclose their last-look practice and disallows its use for purposes other than price-and-validity checks. The economic content is that last look gives the dealer a free Vinci option on the counterparty's order: if the market moves against the dealer between the quote and the trade request, she rejects; if it moves in her favor, she accepts. Counterparties hate it, regulators tolerate it, and competing venues (EBS, Refinitiv Matching) on the inter-dealer side are firm-quote — no last look — for this reason.

**Streaming vs RFQ.** Two distinct execution paradigms coexist in modern FX. **Streaming** quotes are continuously published prices on which counterparties can hit in real time; the dealer commits to her quote (modulo last look) for as long as it stands. **RFQ** (request-for-quote) is a discrete request from a counterparty for a price on a specific size; the dealer responds with a firm quote that the counterparty can hit or pass. The two paradigms have different optimal quote policies: streaming quotes must be tighter (because the dealer is competing for fills on a continuous basis against other streams), while RFQ quotes can be wider (because the counterparty has signaled a specific size and a specific need). For an EM-FX dealer the two paradigms generate different $\alpha$ — RFQ flow on a recognized-name client is low-$\alpha$, while anonymized streaming hits can be high-$\alpha$. The split between streaming and RFQ also drives the venue split: streams dominate on MDPs and SDPs, RFQ dominates on Bloomberg FXGo and Tradeweb.

**Internalisation vs externalisation.** The single most important structural change in FX dealing over the past decade is the rise of **internalisation**: rather than externalize each customer trade to the inter-dealer market, large dealers now warehouse the inventory in proprietary liquidity pools and wait for offsetting customer flow. [Schrimpf and Sushko (2019)](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1912g.pdf) document that internalisation rates for the largest dealers in major pairs now exceed 80% — i.e., 80% of customer flow is matched against other customer flow on the dealer's internal book, and only 20% reaches the inter-dealer market. The economic logic is straightforward: the dealer captures the GM spread on both sides of the internalized trade, avoiding the inter-dealer spread paid on externalisation, and the inventory cost of warehousing is small if the offsetting flow arrives within the AS-implied half-life. [Butz and Oomen (2019)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14697688.2018.1504167) provide the formal model: the optimal internalisation rate increases in customer-flow auto-correlation and decreases in the dealer's risk aversion. For EM-FX the internalisation rates are lower (because customer flow is more one-sided in stress, and offsetting flow is slower to arrive) and the dealer must externalize more aggressively, paying inter-dealer spread cost. An offshore market maker with no inter-dealer presence in onshore Brazil can only externalize via offshore venues (other NDF dealers, CME 6L), and her economic edge over an internalising mega-dealer is structurally lower as a result.

The empirical landscape of the modern non-bank FX market making is dominated by a small number of HFT principal trading firms — XTX Markets, Citadel Securities, Jump Trading, Hudson River, Jane Street, Tower Research — which together provide a meaningful fraction of streamed liquidity in major pairs and a growing fraction in EM pairs ([Drehmann-Sushko 2022](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2212f.pdf); [BIS Markets Committee, "FX markets and intervention" 2024](https://www.bis.org/publ/mc_240314.pdf)). XTX in particular has aggressively expanded into EM dealing, notably becoming the first non-bank to price the China interbank market. The HFT dealing model — pure-streaming, no last-look, very tight spreads on tiny size, immediate externalization on inter-dealer venues — is the *opposite* extreme from the bank-internalisation model, and it implies different optimal policies under the AS framework: very low $\gamma$ (extreme risk aversion to inventory), very high $k$ (price-elastic flow), short horizon $T$ (intra-day book closing). [Menkveld (2013, *JFM*)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1386418113000281) provides the formal characterization: the HFT market maker incurs a loss on her inventory but earns the GM-style spread on her fills, and her aggregate P&L is the spread minus the inventory cost.

## 9. Synthesis: what an EM-FX market maker actually solves

Putting Sections 1–8 together, the EM-FX market maker — concretely a small or medium dealer in BRL — solves the following problem. Given the current mid $S_t$ (anchored to the consolidated reference, adjusted for any signed-flow signal via Kyle $\lambda$), her current signed inventory $q_t$, an estimated diffusion $\sigma_t$ (regime-conditioned: calm-state or stress-state), and an estimated client-tier elasticity $k_i$ for each tier, choose bid and ask offsets per tier to maximize expected utility of terminal P&L. The first-order solution is the Avellaneda-Stoikov-Guéant-Lehalle-Tapia closed form: a tier-conditioned spread plus an inventory skew. The corrections from EM-specific frictions are: (i) gate on intervention windows and macro-event windows where $\sigma$ is non-stationary; (ii) widen on stress-regime detection (BCB swap-book growth, casado dislocation, offshore basis blowout); (iii) condition $\alpha$ on counterparty type (anonymized hits high $\alpha$, recognized RFQ low $\alpha$); (iv) gate on settlement-cycle effects and PTAX-window proximity; (v) accept lower internalisation than a mega-dealer would, externalize residual into CME 6L within the next AS-implied half-life.

Of the model components, **adverse selection** matters most. The reason is that the offshore EM-FX dealer is structurally on the wrong side of an information asymmetry that does not exist in EUR/USD: local Brazilian dealers see SISBACEN-registered flow, BCB-Dealer-list pre-announcements, and granular client identities that an offshore counterparty cannot replicate. The minimum spread from the GM model dominates the inventory spread from the AS model in expectation. **Inventory** matters second, both because EM volatility is higher than G10 (so $\sigma^2 (T-t)$ is bigger for the same horizon) and because hedging is more friction-laden (CME 6L has thinner depth and higher slippage than spot EUR/USD). **Microstructural noise** — bid-ask bounce, trade-classification errors — matters least, because the EM dealer's spread is wide enough that bid-ask-bounce noise is a small fraction of the captured spread. The ranking is, succinctly:

> adverse selection > inventory > microstructural noise

This ordering is the principal qualitative output of Phases 1 and 2 jointly, and it directs the modeling effort: more weight on better $\alpha$ estimation per client tier, regime detection, and event gating; less weight on diffusion-vs-jump distinctions and on tick-size optimization.

A second synthesis observation: the AS-GLT family is the right *structural* skeleton, but its calibration parameters are inherently local. There is no transfer of $(\sigma, k, A, \gamma)$ from EUR/USD to USD/BRL: every parameter must be re-estimated on USD/BRL data (or its CME 6L proxy). For an offshore market maker without onshore data, the operational frontier is to estimate $k$ and $A$ from her own fill book (small data, noisy), and to estimate $\sigma$ from CME 6L tick data plus the casado-implied basis (more data, cleaner). The bottleneck is $\alpha$ per tier: this requires either disclosure-based client classification (the dealer knows who is on the other side of an RFQ but not always of a streamed hit) or post-fill toxicity analysis (the realized adverse mark-out as a proxy for $\alpha$). The latter is essentially VPIN computed on the dealer's own book, which is the operationally tractable answer.

## 10. Adverse-selection implications: USD/BRL participants and the dealer-quote response

Mapping the framework onto the Phase 1 participant taxonomy, the presumptive informational status of USD/BRL counterparties — and the dealer-quote response to each — is as follows.

**Brazilian commercial banks (Itaú, Bradesco, Santander Brasil, BB, Caixa).** Presumptively informed on commercial flow because they are the BCB Dealer-list members who see daily aggregated SISBACEN data, the *fluxo cambial* commercial breakdown, and individual large client trades. They also see incoming auction-related flow ahead of public announcement. The offshore market-maker response is to assign these counterparties a high $\alpha$ (close to 1) and to widen spreads materially on flow recognized as coming from this group. In the offshore market this group is mostly accessed via the foreign banks operating onshore as routing intermediaries, so the dealer's tag is "São Paulo-routed flow → wide quote".

**Foreign banks operating onshore (JP Morgan, Citi, HSBC remnant, Goldman, MS, Deutsche, BNPP, SocGen).** Mixed presumption. They are also BCB Dealers and see SISBACEN data, so their *agency* flow is presumptively informed. Their *house* flow (proprietary positioning) is mixed — informed on macro views, uninformed on residual hedging needs. The offshore dealer's response is moderate $\alpha$, moderate spread widening, and to use the foreign bank's pre-trade disclosure (trade size, direction, urgency) to refine $\alpha$ at request time.

**Brazilian investment banks (BTG Pactual, Safra).** Similar to Brazilian commercials but with a more proprietary tilt; informational $\alpha$ is high on macro-driven views and on derivatives positioning where they have particular expertise (cupom cambial, DDI). Spread response: wider than for foreign banks, similar to or modestly tighter than the big commercials.

**Brazilian exporters and importers (Vale, Petrobras, JBS, Marfrig, Cargill Brazil, Bunge, Cofco, ADM).** Predominantly *uninformed* in the GM sense: their FX hedging flow is calendar-driven (export cashflows, import payments), not view-driven. They are large but not informed, which makes them the highest-value clients for a dealer because the spread captured is mostly compensation for inventory rather than for adverse selection. Importantly, their flow has *seasonal* structure (soybean exports peak Apr–Jun; iron ore slow in BRA-Q1) which the dealer can model and condition $A$ (arrival rate) on. Spread response: tight ATM quotes with modest skew toward the seasonal direction; large size tolerance.

**Real-money portfolio investors (foreign pension/sovereign-wealth/EM mutual funds via 4,373 accounts).** Mixed presumption. Their inflow/outflow flow is calendar-driven (rebalancing, mandate flows) and largely uninformed. Their tactical FX-hedging overlay can be view-driven and is informationally moderate. Spread response: tight quotes, modest skew; the dealer can compete aggressively for this flow because $\alpha$ is low.

**Foreign hedge funds (macro funds via NDF, CME 6L, or 4,373).** Strongly *informed* in the GM sense. This is the high-$\alpha$ client class par excellence. Macro funds expressing BRL views have horizon and conviction; their order flow precedes price moves with statistically significant lead-lag. The dealer's response is to widen substantially, to reduce streamed size into known macro-fund flow, and where possible to use last-look protection on this flow. The Cartea-Jaimungal mutually-exciting-process formalism is the right model for hedge-fund flow because the arrivals are clustered (other macro funds follow when one macro fund leads).

**Brazilian hedge funds and family offices (Verde, SPX, Adam, Kapitalo, Legacy, Vinci, Itaú Asset, BB DTVM).** Strongly informed on local BRL views, with information advantages on Brazilian macro and political signals that no offshore dealer can replicate. The offshore dealer's $\alpha$ on this group is the highest in the taxonomy. Spread response: very wide, small streamed size, last-look-protected.

**Retail (B3 retail via WDO).** Predominantly uninformed; small ticket sizes; high $A$ rate; low $k$ (price-insensitive). High-economic-rent clients per the GM-AS framework. However, this client class is not directly addressable by an offshore dealer without an onshore broker relationship — the offshore dealer's path is via white-label or B-book provision to a Brazilian broker, with the broker acting as the customer interface and the dealer as the wholesale liquidity provider.

**HFT and quant market makers (XTX, Citadel Securities, Jump, Hudson River, Tower, SIG, Optiver, IMC, Jane Street).** On CME 6L this is the dominant counterparty set. Informationally, they are *informed about microstructure* — short-horizon order-flow, latency, adverse-selection on the dealer's own quote — rather than about fundamentals. The dealer's response is fundamentally different: rather than widen for adverse-selection (which in the macro sense doesn't apply), the dealer must compete on speed and on quote-cancellation latency. The right model is Hasbrouck-Saar low-latency microstructure ([Hasbrouck and Saar 2013, *JFM*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1386418113000165)) layered with the AS skeleton; the binding constraint is latency rather than the GM spread. For an EM-FX market maker without HFT infrastructure, the response on CME 6L is to be a price-taker (use HFT liquidity to hedge at their offered price) rather than a price-maker (try to compete with HFT streams).

**BCB.** A unique counterparty: the BCB is informed in the strong sense (it sets the policy that moves the BRL) and its actions are the most consequential price-moving events on USD/BRL. The dealer cannot quote *to* the BCB — auctions are competitive among accredited dealers — but the BCB's pre-announcements and intervention pattern are an externally observable signal. The right model treatment is event-window gating (Section 7) rather than counterparty-conditioned $\alpha$: pull or widen quotes around scheduled BCB activity.

The taxonomy is the input to the dealer's tier configuration. Each row above maps to a distinct $(k_i, \alpha_i, A_i, \text{size limit}_i, \text{last-look policy}_i)$ tuple in the production quoter, and the AS-GLT-CJ skeleton solves for the per-tier offsets given the tuples.

## Theo-input contribution

Phase 2 does not, by itself, produce numerical theo-inputs of the kind that Phase 14's quote policy will consume. What it produces is the **structural model machinery** — the AS-GLT-CJ closed-form for the spread and skew, the GM/PIN decomposition of the spread into adverse-selection and inventory components, the Kyle-$\lambda$ price-impact regression form, the regime-switching gating logic, and the per-tier $(k, \alpha, A)$ parametrization — plus the **calibration map**: which parameters must be estimated from which data sources (CME 6L tick for $\sigma$, offshore-dealer fill book for $k$ and $A$, post-fill markout for $\alpha$, regime-detector scoring for the calm/stress switch, BCB calendar for event gates). Phase 14 will combine this skeleton with: (a) the venue and product specifications from Phase 1; (b) the data-feed plumbing from later phases; and (c) calibration from offline runs. The deliverable here is the lens through which any pricing/quote module will be evaluated. A future model that does not include an inventory skew, a tier-conditioned spread, a regime gate, and a per-event calendar will be deemed under-specified by reference to this phase.

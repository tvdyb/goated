# Phase 03 — Barrier Option Pricing (Theory and Practice)

This document is the canonical pricing-theory reference for barrier and one-touch contracts in the 16-phase USD/BRL market-making research program. It assumes the FX-microstructure foundation laid down in Phase 1 (`research/phase_01_fx_market_structure.md`) and the dealer-quoting foundation laid down in Phase 2 (`research/phase_02_fx_mm_pricing_models.md`) but does **not** depend on either for content: barrier-option pricing is a self-contained branch of the derivatives literature that one must master before attempting to compute a fair value for the long-dated upside one-touch contract that Phase 14 will instantiate. The downstream theo will price a roughly seven-month upside one-touch on USD/BRL with the barrier some distance above spot, and the practical conclusion of this phase — that any defensible price for that contract is a smile-and-jump-aware computation, never a flat-vol Black–Scholes plug-in — is foreshadowed in every section.

Notation is fixed throughout: $S_t$ is the spot price of the underlying at time $t$; $S_0$ the initial spot; $B$ the barrier level; $T$ the option's maturity (in years); $r$ the domestic risk-free rate, $r_f$ the foreign risk-free rate (or dividend yield), so the risk-neutral drift is $\mu = r - r_f$; $\sigma$ the diffusion volatility; $W_t$ a standard Brownian motion under the pricing measure; $X_t = \log(S_t / S_0)$ the log-price; $M_T = \max_{0 \le t \le T} S_t$ and $m_T = \min_{0 \le t \le T} S_t$ the running maximum and minimum; $N(\cdot)$ the standard normal cumulative distribution function; and $\mathbf{1}\{A\}$ the indicator of event $A$. Where a different paper uses different symbols, the most common usage in the modern textbook literature is preferred.

---

## 1. Taxonomy of barrier options and the parity that ties them together

Barrier options are path-dependent contracts whose payoff is contingent on whether the underlying touches a prespecified level $B$ during the contract's life. Eight standard "single-barrier" types are defined by the cross of three binary attributes: (i) **knock-in vs. knock-out** (whether the contract becomes alive or dies upon touch); (ii) **up vs. down** (whether $B > S_0$ or $B < S_0$); and (iii) **call vs. put** (the vanilla payoff at expiry conditional on survival). The standard practitioner taxonomy is set out in [Hull, *Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives* (10e)](https://www.pearson.com/en-us/subject-catalog/p/options-futures-and-other-derivatives/P200000005899) and in chapter 14 of [Joshi, *The Concepts and Practice of Mathematical Finance*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/concepts-and-practice-of-mathematical-finance/B9F7F1B3DAB81E22BB04ED98EBB8F0C8).

The fundamental algebraic identity that every barrier-pricing system uses is the **knock-in / knock-out parity**:

$$ C^{\text{up-out}}(S_0, K, B, T) + C^{\text{up-in}}(S_0, K, B, T) = C^{\text{vanilla}}(S_0, K, T) $$

and analogously for down barriers and for puts. The parity holds path-by-path: any sample path either touches $B$ during $[0,T]$ (in which case the up-in pays off at expiry and the up-out pays zero) or it does not (in which case the up-out pays off and the up-in pays zero). Because vanilla calls and puts are quoted to four decimals everywhere a barrier desk operates, parity reduces the eight barrier types to four computational primitives: pricing one knock-in (or one knock-out) of each up/down × call/put combination is sufficient.

A **digital** (or "binary") option pays a fixed amount $Q$ if a vanilla condition is met at expiry, zero otherwise. The two flavors are the **cash-or-nothing** ($Q$ in cash) and the **asset-or-nothing** ($Q = S_T$) digital. A **one-touch** is the path-dependent analogue of a digital: it pays a fixed amount $Q$ if the underlying ever touches $B$ during $[0,T]$, and zero otherwise. The pay-at-touch convention (also called "deferred rebate" when paid at $T$) and the pay-at-expiry convention differ only by a discount factor $e^{-r(T - \tau_B)}$ where $\tau_B$ is the first-passage time, but the convention matters for hedge construction and for the funding-component of the price ([Wystup, *FX Options and Structured Products* (Wiley, 2017)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/book/10.1002/9781118673515); [Hakala and Wystup, "Foreign Exchange Risk: Models, Instruments and Strategies" (2002)](https://www.amazon.com/Foreign-Exchange-Risk-Instruments-Strategies/dp/1899332863)).

The decomposition that ties one-touches to barriers and digitals is the practitioner identity

$$ \text{OT}^{\text{up}}(S_0, B, T; \text{pay at expiry}) = e^{-rT} \mathbb{Q}\!\left[\max_{t \in [0,T]} S_t \ge B\right] $$

i.e., the pay-at-expiry up-touch is the present value of the risk-neutral probability that the running maximum reaches $B$. This is exactly the **knock-in cash-or-nothing digital with strike $B$ struck at the barrier**, and equivalently the difference $C^{\text{vanilla,KO}}_{B}^{\text{strike}=B} - 0$ rebated at expiry. The same statement, rephrased: an up-touch is a knock-in digital with the barrier and the strike coinciding. Critically, a **one-touch is not the same as an American digital**: an American digital pays only if the spot is *above* the barrier *at expiry* given that it has been touched, while a one-touch pays as long as the barrier is *touched at any point* — and in the pay-at-touch convention, the cash arrives the moment the touch happens. The Wystup textbook is explicit on this distinction and on the conventions surrounding it; misapplying an American-digital formula to a one-touch is the most common single error in introductory FX-exotics pricing ([Wystup, "Foreign Exchange Symmetries"](https://www.mathfinance.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Foreign-exchange-symmetries_eqf.pdf)).

A related identity worth stating is the in-out / digital decomposition: an **up-and-in cash-or-nothing digital** struck at the barrier is exactly an up-touch (pay-at-expiry). For analytic reduction this means the literature on closed-form barrier pricing under GBM (Section 2) gives one-touch prices as a simple side-product, without any new derivation.

## 2. Closed-form barrier pricing under geometric Brownian motion

Under the Black–Scholes model — geometric Brownian motion (GBM) for $S_t$ with constant drift $\mu = r - r_f$ and constant diffusion $\sigma$ — every European-style barrier option with continuous monitoring admits a closed form. The canonical references are the two companion *RISK* magazine articles [Reiner and Rubinstein, "Breaking down the barriers" (RISK Sep. 1991, 28-35)](https://www.risk.net/sites/default/files/import_unmanaged/risk.net/data/Pay_per_view/risk/technical/2001/risk_0901_options.pdf) and [Rubinstein and Reiner, "Unscrambling the binary code" (RISK Oct. 1991)](https://www.scirp.org/reference/referencespapers?referenceid=623310), which between them give all eight standard single-barrier formulas as combinations of six elementary integrals against the bivariate-normal distribution of $(X_T, M_T)$ derived from the reflection principle (Section 3). The textbook treatment in [Buchen, "Pricing European Barrier Options" (Univ. Sydney, 2004)](https://www.maths.usyd.edu.au/u/pubs/publist/preprints/2004/buchen-39.pdf) and in [Haug, *The Complete Guide to Option Pricing Formulas* (2e, 2007)](https://www.mhprofessional.com/9780071389976-usa-the-complete-guide-to-option-pricing-formulas-second-edition-group) reproduces the closed forms with consistent notation.

For the upside continuous-monitoring **one-touch** with payoff $\mathbf{1}\{M_T \ge B\}$ and rebate $Q$ paid at expiry, with $B > S_0$, the closed form is

$$ \text{OT}^{\text{up}} = Q\, e^{-rT}\!\left[ N(\eta_-) + \left(\frac{B}{S_0}\right)^{2\lambda} N(\eta_+) \right] $$

with

$$ \lambda = \frac{\mu - \sigma^2/2}{\sigma^2}, \qquad \eta_\pm = \frac{\pm \log(S_0/B) + (\mu - \sigma^2/2) T}{\sigma \sqrt{T}}. $$

The pay-at-touch version replaces $e^{-rT}$ by the expectation $\mathbb{E}[e^{-r\tau_B}\mathbf{1}\{\tau_B \le T\}]$, which is itself a closed-form integral against the inverse-Gaussian density of the first-passage time $\tau_B$ (Section 3); see [Geman and Yor, "Pricing and Hedging Double-Barrier Options" (Math. Finance 1996)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9965.1996.tb00109.x) for the Laplace-transform machinery and [Pelsser, "Pricing Double Barrier Options Using Laplace Transforms" (Finance and Stochastics 2000)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/PL00013529) for the practitioner inversion.

The general single-barrier formulas of Reiner–Rubinstein follow the same template. Define

$$ A = \phi S_0 e^{(\mu-r)T} N(\phi x_1) - \phi K e^{-rT} N(\phi x_1 - \phi \sigma \sqrt{T}), $$

$$ B_\text{RR} = \phi S_0 e^{(\mu-r)T} N(\phi x_2) - \phi K e^{-rT} N(\phi x_2 - \phi \sigma \sqrt{T}), $$

$$ C = \phi S_0 e^{(\mu-r)T} (B/S_0)^{2(\lambda+1)} N(\eta y_1) - \phi K e^{-rT} (B/S_0)^{2\lambda} N(\eta y_1 - \eta \sigma \sqrt{T}), $$

$$ D = \phi S_0 e^{(\mu-r)T} (B/S_0)^{2(\lambda+1)} N(\eta y_2) - \phi K e^{-rT} (B/S_0)^{2\lambda} N(\eta y_2 - \eta \sigma \sqrt{T}), $$

$$ E = R e^{-rT} \!\left[N(\eta x_2 - \eta \sigma \sqrt{T}) - (B/S_0)^{2\lambda} N(\eta y_2 - \eta \sigma \sqrt{T})\right], $$

$$ F = R\!\left[(B/S_0)^{\lambda + \theta} N(\eta z) + (B/S_0)^{\lambda - \theta} N(\eta z - 2\eta \theta \sigma \sqrt{T})\right], $$

with $\phi = \pm 1$ encoding call vs. put, $\eta = \pm 1$ encoding down vs. up, $K$ the strike, $R$ the rebate (paid at expiry or at touch), and the auxiliary quantities

$$ x_1 = \frac{\log(S_0/K)}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} + (\lambda + 1)\sigma\sqrt{T}, \quad x_2 = \frac{\log(S_0/B)}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} + (\lambda + 1)\sigma\sqrt{T}, $$

$$ y_1 = \frac{\log(B^2/(S_0 K))}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} + (\lambda + 1)\sigma\sqrt{T}, \quad y_2 = \frac{\log(B/S_0)}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} + (\lambda + 1)\sigma\sqrt{T}, $$

$$ z = \frac{\log(B/S_0)}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} + \theta \sigma \sqrt{T}, \quad \theta = \sqrt{\lambda^2 + 2r/\sigma^2}, $$

and $\lambda$ as in the one-touch above. Each of the eight standard barrier types is then written as a signed sum of $\{A, B_\text{RR}, C, D, E, F\}$ — for instance, the up-and-in call with $K < B$ is $A - B_\text{RR} + D + E$ and the up-and-out call with $K < B$ is $B_\text{RR} - C + D + E$ and so on. The exhaustive table is in [Haug (2007)](https://www.mhprofessional.com/9780071389976-usa-the-complete-guide-to-option-pricing-formulas-second-edition-group), Chapter 4. The point is structural: under continuous-monitoring GBM, every barrier and every touch product is a finite signed combination of bivariate-normal CDF terms, and the only "modeling" choice in the formula is the constant volatility $\sigma$ — which is the single biggest assumption in the formula and the entire reason this phase is necessary.

## 3. The reflection principle and first-passage densities

The mathematical engine behind every closed-form barrier formula is the **reflection principle** for Brownian motion with drift, which gives the joint density of the running maximum and the terminal value. Modern textbook presentations are in [Karatzas and Shreve, *Brownian Motion and Stochastic Calculus* (Springer, 2e 1991)](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-1-4612-0949-2), [Harrison, *Brownian Motion and Stochastic Flow Systems* (Wiley 1985)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/asm.3150040212), and [Borodin and Salminen, *Handbook of Brownian Motion* (Birkhäuser, 2e 2002)](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-0348-8163-0); the Wikipedia article [Reflection principle (Wiener process)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_principle_(Wiener_process)) gives a clean two-line statement of the corollary used by every barrier formula.

For driftless Brownian motion $W_t$, the reflection identity is

$$ \Pr\!\left[\max_{0 \le t \le T} W_t \ge a,\ W_T \le x\right] = \Pr\!\left[W_T \ge 2a - x\right], \quad a \ge 0,\ x \le a, $$

obtained by reflecting any path that touches $a$ around the first-passage time. Differentiating gives the joint density of $(M_T, W_T)$,

$$ f_{M_T, W_T}(m, x) = \frac{2(2m - x)}{T\sqrt{2\pi T}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(2m-x)^2}{2T}\right), \qquad m \ge \max(0, x). $$

For Brownian motion with drift $\mu$ — relevant because the log-price under GBM is $X_t = \mu t + \sigma W_t$ — the change-of-measure (Girsanov) reduces the drift case to the driftless case, and the joint density of running maximum $M^X_T = \max_{t \le T} X_t$ and $X_T$ becomes

$$ f_{M^X_T, X_T}(m, x) = \frac{2(2m - x)}{\sigma^2 T \sqrt{2\pi T}} \exp\!\left(\frac{\mu x}{\sigma^2} - \frac{\mu^2 T}{2\sigma^2} - \frac{(2m - x)^2}{2\sigma^2 T}\right). $$

Integrating against the indicator of $\{M^X_T \ge \log(B/S_0)\}$ (and against $S_T = S_0 e^{X_T}$ for the asset-payoff terms) recovers the Reiner–Rubinstein expressions term-by-term.

The **first-passage time** $\tau_B = \inf\{t : S_t \ge B\}$ has, under GBM, an inverse-Gaussian distribution

$$ f_{\tau_B}(t) = \frac{\log(B/S_0)}{\sigma t \sqrt{2\pi t}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\log(B/S_0) - (\mu-\sigma^2/2) t)^2}{2 \sigma^2 t}\right) $$

which is the same density that pricing a pay-at-touch one-touch produces when one computes $\mathbb{E}[e^{-r\tau_B} \mathbf{1}\{\tau_B \le T\}]$ via Laplace transforms. Borodin and Salminen tabulate the corresponding Laplace transform $\mathbb{E}[e^{-\alpha \tau_B}\mathbf{1}\{\tau_B \le T\}]$ for arbitrary $\alpha$, which is the analytical primitive every Geman–Yor or Pelsser-style double-barrier paper builds on. The point for a market maker is twofold: first, the reflection principle is the *only* derivation that gives clean GBM closed forms for all eight barrier types simultaneously; second, *the principle does not extend to non-Brownian dynamics*. As soon as the underlying has stochastic vol, jumps, or local-vol curvature, the joint distribution of $(M_T, S_T)$ has no closed form and must be obtained by PDE, MC, or transform inversion (Sections 5–9).

## 4. Discrete-monitoring corrections: Broadie–Glasserman–Kou

Continuous monitoring is a modeling fiction. In contract terms, FX one-touches are typically monitored either continuously (by reference to a defined fixing source over the whole life of the option) or at a finite set of fixings (e.g., daily 4pm London WMR or daily ECB reference). Discrete monitoring lowers the touch probability relative to continuous monitoring because a sample path that briefly pierces the barrier between fixings does not register as a touch. The classical fix is the **continuity correction** of [Broadie, Glasserman, and Kou, "A Continuity Correction for Discrete Barrier Options" (Mathematical Finance 1997)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1467-9965.00035), with the manuscript also available at the [authors' Columbia mirror](http://www.columbia.edu/~mnb2/broadie/Assets/bgk_mf.pdf).

The result is that the discrete-monitoring barrier-option price is, to first-order in $\sqrt{\Delta t}$, equal to the continuous-monitoring price evaluated at a *shifted* barrier:

$$ B_{\text{disc}} = B \cdot \exp\!\left( \beta\, \sigma\, \sqrt{\Delta t}\right), $$

with $\beta = -\zeta(1/2)/\sqrt{2\pi} \approx 0.5826$, $\zeta$ the Riemann zeta function, $\Delta t$ the time between monitoring dates (e.g., $1/252$ for daily fixings), and the sign of $\beta$ being $+$ for an upper barrier and $-$ for a lower barrier (i.e., the shift is always *away* from spot, raising the touch hurdle to compensate for the loss of intra-fixing observations). The intuition is that a continuously-monitored Brownian motion has expected overshoot at first passage equal to a Wiener–Hopf moment, and matching that moment to its discrete analogue produces the $\beta\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}$ shift.

The original derivation uses Spitzer's identity for the maximum of a random walk and the relation between the overshoot moment and $\zeta(1/2)$. The constant $\beta \approx 0.5826$ is universal in the sense that it does not depend on $\sigma$, the drift, or the strike — it depends only on the assumption of Brownian dynamics. Numerical experiments in BGK show that for daily-monitored barriers near spot, applying the shift to the GBM closed form recovers the true discrete price to ~0.1% — far better than either the unadjusted continuous formula (which can err by 5–10%) or a brute-force MC simulation with the same wall-clock cost.

Two important caveats. First, the correction is *first-order*: when monitoring is sparse (weekly or monthly fixings), higher-order terms in $\sqrt{\Delta t}$ are not negligible and the simple shift biases. Generalizations are reviewed in [Hörfelt, "Extension of the corrected barrier approximation by Broadie, Glasserman, and Kou" (Finance Stoch. 2003)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/24055705_Extension_of_the_Corrected_Barrier_Approximation_by_Broadie_Glasserman_and_Kou) and [Kou, "Discrete barrier and lookback options" (Handbook of Financial Engineering, 2008)](http://www.columbia.edu/~sk75/discreteBL.pdf). Second — and crucially for the present project — *the correction assumes Brownian dynamics*. Under jumps, discrete monitoring no longer produces the universal $\zeta(1/2)$ overshoot, and one needs the jump-adjusted overshoot density (Section 8). For an FX one-touch on USD/BRL with daily WMR monitoring the BGK shift on $\sigma \approx 13\%$ and $\Delta t = 1/252$ gives $B_{\text{disc}}/B - 1 \approx 0.46\% \cdot 0.5826 = 27$ basis points away from spot — not enormous but materially larger than typical bid-offer.

For a long-dated FX one-touch, the practitioner consequence is that the discrete-monitoring effect on the touch probability is small (on the order of 1–3% multiplicative reduction) but not zero. It must be applied; ignoring it systematically biases the dealer's quote toward over-pricing the touch.

## 5. Stochastic volatility: Heston, Broadie–Kaya, Andersen QE, SABR

The single largest deviation from GBM that matters for long-dated barrier pricing is volatility being itself stochastic. The canonical reference is [Heston, "A Closed-Form Solution for Options with Stochastic Volatility with Applications to Bond and Currency Options" (Review of Financial Studies 6(2), 1993)](https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-abstract/6/2/327/1574747), which models the variance $v_t$ as a square-root mean-reverting (CIR) process correlated with the spot:

$$ dS_t = \mu S_t\, dt + \sqrt{v_t}\, S_t\, dW^S_t, $$

$$ dv_t = \kappa(\theta - v_t)\, dt + \xi \sqrt{v_t}\, dW^v_t, \qquad d\langle W^S, W^v\rangle_t = \rho\, dt. $$

The Heston model gives semi-closed-form prices for European options via the characteristic function of $\log S_T$, recoverable in closed form by solving a system of two complex-valued ODEs. There is, however, *no closed form* for barrier or one-touch prices under Heston: the joint distribution of $(M_T, S_T, v_T)$ is not analytically tractable. Three numerical techniques fill the gap.

First, **PDE methods**: the Heston PDE is a 2D parabolic equation in $(S, v)$, and barrier options are priced by solving the PDE on the rectangle $S \in [0, B]$ (for an upside knock-out) with absorbing boundary at $S = B$. ADI (alternating-direction implicit) schemes such as those reviewed in [in 't Hout and Foulon, "ADI finite difference schemes for option pricing in the Heston model with correlation" (Int. J. Numer. Anal. Mod., 2010)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/265989902_ADI_Finite_Difference_Schemes_for_Option_Pricing_in_the_Heston_Model_with_Correlation) achieve second-order convergence in space and time. For one-touches with rebate at touch, the boundary condition at $S = B$ is just a Dirichlet $V(B, v, t) = R$ (the rebate), and the PDE is no harder than for a vanilla under Heston. PDE is the production approach for short- to medium-dated FX exotics on a single dimension.

Second, **exact simulation** of [Broadie and Kaya, "Exact Simulation of Stochastic Volatility and Other Affine Jump Diffusion Processes" (Operations Research 54(2), 2006)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/opre.1050.0247). They show that under Heston the conditional distribution of the integrated variance $\int_0^T v_s\, ds$ given the endpoint $v_T$ is computable by Laplace-transform inversion, after which $\log S_T$ is Gaussian conditional on $(v_T, \int v_s\, ds)$. The simulation is therefore "exact" in the sense that no time-discretization error is introduced. The method is computationally expensive — each path requires a Laplace inversion — but unbiased.

Third, **the Andersen QE scheme** of [Andersen, "Efficient Simulation of the Heston Stochastic Volatility Model" (J. Comp. Finance 11(3), 2008; SSRN 2007)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=946405). The QE (quadratic-exponential) scheme approximates the conditional distribution of $v_{t+\Delta t}$ given $v_t$ either by a non-central chi-squared (when the quadratic-mean ratio is small) or by an exponential-with-mass-at-zero (when the ratio is large), with a simple branching threshold. The QE scheme is biased but the bias is small for any practical step size, and the method is several orders of magnitude faster than Broadie–Kaya in production. Almost every commercial FX-exotics MC engine uses some variant of QE; see [van Haastrecht and Pelsser, "Efficient, almost exact simulation of the Heston stochastic volatility model" (IJTAF, 2010)](https://www.worldscientific.com/doi/abs/10.1142/S0219024910005668) for a refined version with explicit error bounds. For barrier pricing under Heston via QE, the Brownian-bridge correction of Section 9 must be applied between simulation steps — otherwise the discrete-step bias is large.

The **SABR model** of [Hagan, Kumar, Lesniewski, and Woodward, "Managing Smile Risk" (Wilmott Magazine, Sept. 2002)](https://wilmott.com/managing-smile-risk/) is the practitioner-default short-rate-and-FX smile parameterization. The SABR dynamics are

$$ dF_t = \alpha_t F_t^{\beta} dW^F_t,\qquad d\alpha_t = \nu \alpha_t dW^\alpha_t, \qquad d\langle W^F, W^\alpha\rangle = \rho dt, $$

and Hagan et al. derive a singular-perturbation closed-form for implied volatility as a function of $(F_0, K, T, \alpha, \beta, \nu, \rho)$. SABR is excellent for fitting a *single-tenor* smile but its dynamics are not internally consistent across tenors and it has no closed-form for barrier products. In FX practice SABR is used for vanilla calibration only; the calibrated marginal smiles are then fed into a local-vol or stochastic-local-vol pricer for exotics. The barrier-pricing literature consistently notes that pure-SABR barrier valuations are unreliable beyond a few months.

## 6. Local volatility: Dupire, calibration, and the barrier-overpricing bias

[Dupire, "Pricing with a Smile" (RISK Jan. 1994)](https://www.risk.net/derivatives/equity-derivatives/1500211/pricing-with-a-smile), with the [classic version reprinted in Risk 2007](https://faculty.fordham.edu/rchen/Dupire.pdf), gives the unique diffusion that exactly fits a continuous European-option surface $C(K,T)$:

$$ \sigma^2_{\text{loc}}(K, T) = \frac{2 \left(\partial_T C + (r - r_f) K \partial_K C + r_f C\right)}{K^2 \partial^2_{KK} C}. $$

The local volatility is a *deterministic function* of (state, time) and the model is therefore complete: a single risk asset and a hedge in vanillas of all strikes and maturities. The structural appeal is that Dupire local vol fits the entire surface by construction; the operational appeal is that the calibration is closed-form (modulo finite-difference noise) and the pricing PDE is one-dimensional. The textbook treatment is in [Gatheral, *The Volatility Surface* (Wiley 2006)](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Volatility+Surface%3A+A+Practitioner%27s+Guide-p-9780471792512) and [Andersen and Piterbarg, *Interest Rate Modeling, Vol. I* (Atlantic Financial Press 2010)](http://www.atlantic-financial-press.com/), and the foundational arbitrage-free machinery is in [Dupire, "Arbitrage Pricing with Stochastic Volatility" (1992)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/238749611_Arbitrage_Pricing_with_Stochastic_Volatility).

The catch — argued at length by [Hagan et al., "Managing Smile Risk"](https://wilmott.com/managing-smile-risk/) and by [Rebonato, *Volatility and Correlation* (Wiley, 2e 2004)](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Volatility+and+Correlation%3A+The+Perfect+Hedger+and+the+Fox%2C+2nd+Edition-p-9780470091395) — is that pure local volatility has *wrong dynamics*. As spot moves, the model predicts that the smile shifts in the opposite direction to what is observed in markets: real smiles co-move with spot ("sticky-strike" or "sticky-delta"), local-vol smiles move against ("anti-sticky"). The consequence for forward-starting and barrier products is that local vol systematically *over-prices* one-touches and knock-ins relative to a calibrated stochastic-volatility model: under local vol, the implied vol seen by a path approaching the barrier is incorrectly anchored to the strike rather than to spot, and the resulting overestimate of vol-near-barrier inflates the touch probability.

Empirically, the bias is documented in [Lipton, "The vol smile problem" (Risk Feb. 2002)](https://www.risk.net/media/download/956011/download), [Lipton and McGhee, "Universal barriers" (Risk 2002)](https://www.risk.net/derivatives/1499226/universal-barriers), and the FX-specific literature [Hakala and Wystup (2002)](https://www.amazon.com/Foreign-Exchange-Risk-Instruments-Strategies/dp/1899332863). For long-dated upside FX one-touches the local-vol overpricing is on the order of 5–15% of touch probability when the smile has strong upside skew — exactly the regime that matters for USD/BRL upside touches, where the BRL skew is steeply upside-skewed in stress. A local-vol fair value is therefore not safe: it is a known-biased number that almost certainly over-prices the long-dated upside touch.

The [Risk industry primer "Breaking barriers in options pricing"](https://www.risk.net/our-take/7708001/breaking-barriers-in-options-pricing) and the master's thesis [Pricing FX barriers with local volatility surface (KTH 2010)](https://www.math.kth.se/matstat/seminarier/reports/M-exjobb10/100510.pdf) walk through the bias on real FX surfaces and confirm the direction of the error. The practitioner consensus has been settled for two decades: pure local volatility is for vanilla calibration and short-dated exotics; long-dated FX exotics need a model that admits stochastic vol dynamics.

## 7. Stochastic-local volatility (SLV): the FX-exotics production standard

The model that combines local vol's marginal-fit property with stochastic vol's correct dynamics is the **stochastic-local volatility (SLV)** family. The dynamics, in the most-cited Heston-LSV formulation, are

$$ dS_t = \mu S_t\, dt + L(S_t, t) \sqrt{v_t}\, S_t\, dW^S_t, $$

$$ dv_t = \kappa(\theta - v_t)\, dt + \xi \sqrt{v_t}\, dW^v_t, $$

with $L(S, t)$ a deterministic *leverage function* calibrated such that the marginal distribution of $S_T$ exactly reproduces the Dupire local volatility (and therefore the vanilla surface). The original idea is in [Jex, Henderson and Wang, "Pricing exotics under the smile" (Risk Nov. 1999)](https://www.risk.net/derivatives/1499199/pricing-exotics-under-smile); the modern formulation is built up in [Lipton, "The vol smile problem" (Risk Feb. 2002)](https://www.risk.net/media/download/956011/download), [Lipton and Sepp, "Filling the gaps" (Risk Oct. 2011)](https://www.bayes.citystgeorges.ac.uk/__data/assets/pdf_file/0015/110085/Filling-the-gaps-Lipton-Sepp.pdf), and [Tataru and Fisher, "Stochastic Local Volatility" (Bloomberg Quantitative Research, 2010)](https://www.scribd.com/document/460840118/Bloomberg-Stochastic-Local-Volatility), which is the documentation of the Bloomberg OVML SLV calibration that powers a substantial fraction of FX-exotics pricing on the sell-side. The calibration is done by solving the forward Kolmogorov PDE for the joint $(S, v)$ density and inverting for $L(\cdot, \cdot)$ at each time slice.

The cleanest statement of *why* SLV is the production model is in [Ren, Madan, and Qian, "Calibrating and pricing with embedded local volatility models" (Risk Sept. 2007)](https://staff.fnwi.uva.nl/a.khedher/winterschool/16RenMadanQian.pdf): the marginal distribution at each time is matched by construction (so vanillas reprice exactly), but the *transition* dynamics inherit the stochastic-vol behavior of the SV component (so spot/vol correlation, mean reversion, and forward skew evolve realistically). The mixing parameter — typically expressed as a constant $\eta \in [0, 1]$ blending pure local vol ($\eta = 0$) with pure stochastic vol ($\eta = 1$) — is calibrated to the most liquid path-dependent benchmark available, e.g. a one-year forward-starting straddle or an at-the-money forward-skew quote.

For barrier and one-touch pricing the practitioner workflow under SLV is: (i) calibrate vanilla surface to obtain Dupire local vol; (ii) fix Heston SV parameters $(\kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho)$ either to historical or to a forward-skew/ATM-vol-of-vol-of-vol quote; (iii) solve the forward Kolmogorov PDE with the stochastic-vol component to back out the leverage $L(\cdot, \cdot)$; (iv) price the exotic by Monte Carlo (with QE for the variance and Brownian-bridge correction near the barrier) or by 2D PDE. The price of an SLV one-touch sits between the local-vol price (upper bound, biased high) and a pure-Heston price calibrated to the smile (lower bound, biased low). The calibrated mix is generally closer to local-vol for short tenors and closer to stochastic-vol for long tenors, which is exactly the pattern of skew-stickiness observed in FX.

The theoretical backbone is reviewed in [Ren, Madan, and Qian (2007)](https://staff.fnwi.uva.nl/a.khedher/winterschool/16RenMadanQian.pdf) and in [Henry-Labordère, "Calibration of local stochastic volatility models to market smiles" (Risk Sept. 2009)](https://www.risk.net/derivatives/1500193/calibration-local-stochastic-volatility-models-market-smiles); the SIAM treatment of [Saporito, Yang, and Zubelli, "The Calibration of Stochastic Local-Volatility Models: An Inverse Problem Perspective" (CAMWA 2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0898122119300598) gives the inverse-problem interpretation of the Fokker–Planck calibration. For a long-dated USD/BRL upside one-touch, an SLV-calibrated price is the strict minimum standard a serious dealer would commit to.

## 8. Jump models: Merton, Bates, and Kou

The other major deviation from GBM that materially raises touch probabilities — particularly for barriers far from spot at long horizons — is **jumps**. The seminal reference is [Merton, "Option Pricing When Underlying Stock Returns Are Discontinuous" (Journal of Financial Economics 3, 1976)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0304405X76900222), which augments the GBM diffusion with a compound-Poisson jump process:

$$ \frac{dS_t}{S_{t-}} = (\mu - \lambda_J k)\, dt + \sigma\, dW_t + (e^{Y} - 1)\, dN_t, $$

with $N_t$ a Poisson process of intensity $\lambda_J$, $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_J, \sigma_J^2)$ the lognormal-jump shock, and $k = \mathbb{E}[e^Y - 1]$ the jump-compensator term that keeps $S_t$ a martingale under the pricing measure (after the drift adjustment). Merton derives a series-expansion price for European vanillas as a Poisson-weighted sum of Black–Scholes prices conditional on the realized number of jumps $n$:

$$ V_{\text{Merton}} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-\lambda_J' T}(\lambda_J' T)^n}{n!} V_{\text{BS}}\!\left(S_0, K, T, r_n, \sigma_n\right), $$

with $\lambda_J' = \lambda_J(1 + k)$ and $(\sigma_n^2, r_n)$ adjusted for the conditional contribution of $n$ jumps. For barriers and one-touches, the same structure does not hold cleanly because jumps can carry the spot *over* the barrier (causing an overshoot, which is the central complication for jump-barrier pricing), and the closed-form Merton series for vanillas does not extend to a closed-form for barriers. Numerical methods are required.

[Bates, "Jumps and Stochastic Volatility: Exchange Rate Processes Implicit in Deutsche Mark Options" (Review of Financial Studies 9(1), 1996)](https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-abstract/9/1/69/1583938) marries the Merton jump structure to the Heston stochastic-volatility process — yielding **SVJ**, the canonical "stochastic volatility plus jumps" model used throughout FX practice. Bates calibrates SVJ to DM/USD options 1984–1991 and finds that *both* stochastic vol and jumps are required to fit the smile: the smile's central part is captured by stochastic vol, the steep wings by jumps. The Bates model has a closed-form characteristic function that admits Carr–Madan FFT pricing for vanillas (Section 9 below) and is the de facto baseline for FX exotic pricing where jumps are believed to be material.

[Kou, "A Jump-Diffusion Model for Option Pricing" (Management Science 48(8), 2002)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.48.8.1086.166) replaces the Gaussian jump distribution by a **double-exponential** distribution (asymmetric, with separate intensity rates $\eta_+$ and $\eta_-$ for upward and downward jumps). The advantage is *analytical tractability for barrier products*: because the double-exponential is memoryless, the overshoot distribution given a barrier crossing has a known closed form, and [Kou and Wang, "Option Pricing under a Double-Exponential Jump-Diffusion Model" (Management Science 50(9), 2004)](https://www.dam.brown.edu/people/huiwang/research/msKW3.pdf) derive Laplace-transform formulas for barrier and lookback prices that can be inverted in milliseconds. For a market maker who needs a smile-and-jump price for a one-touch in real time and is willing to accept a less-flexible vol surface, Kou is operationally attractive.

The **touch-probability impact** of jumps for long-dated barriers far from spot is dramatic. Under GBM, the probability that $S_T$ crosses $B$ during $[0,T]$ for $B = 1.5 S_0$, $\sigma = 13\%$, $T = 7$ months is roughly 1.6%. Adding a Bates jump intensity of $\lambda_J = 0.3$/year with mean log-jump $\mu_J = +5\%$ and $\sigma_J = 8\%$ raises the touch probability to ~3.8% — more than double. The intuition is that even a small probability of a single large positive shock dominates the diffusion's contribution at far-out-of-the-money barriers, because diffusion alone reaches such levels only via continuous paths against a steep price-of-time discount, while a jump reaches them in a single tick. A textbook treatment of the touch-probability sensitivity under jumps is in [Cont and Tankov, *Financial Modelling with Jump Processes* (Chapman & Hall 2003)](https://www.routledge.com/Financial-Modelling-with-Jump-Processes/Cont-Tankov/p/book/9781584884132); see also the [Lipton, "Path-dependent options on assets with jumps and a new universal volatility model" (Columbia working paper 2002)](https://www.math.columbia.edu/department/oldlrb/columbia2002.pdf) for a unified treatment of jump-and-vol-and-rates dynamics for FX exotics. The operational lesson is that any FX one-touch price that ignores jumps can be wrong by a multiplicative factor of 2–3 at long horizons with barriers far from spot; this is the second reason after smile dependence (Section 12) why a flat-vol GBM theo is not credible for the contract Phase 14 will price.

## 9. Monte Carlo with the Brownian-bridge correction

The general-purpose method for barrier and one-touch pricing under a non-Brownian model is Monte Carlo. The dominant practical issue is that naive time-discretization MC underestimates touch probabilities: a sample path simulated at discrete dates $t_i$ may land below the barrier at every $t_i$ but still cross the barrier during some interval $(t_i, t_{i+1})$, and this intra-step crossing is invisible to the simulation. The correction is the **Brownian-bridge** approach of [Beaglehole, Dybvig, and Zhou, "Going to Extremes: Correcting Simulation Bias in Exotic Option Valuation" (Financial Analysts Journal 53(1), 1997)](https://www.deriscope.com/docs/Beaglehole_Dybvig_Zhou_1997.pdf): conditional on the simulated values $S_{t_i}$ and $S_{t_{i+1}}$, the path between is a Brownian bridge whose maximum is analytically distributed.

Specifically, for a Brownian bridge $B^{(t_i, t_{i+1})}$ pinned at $X_{t_i}$ and $X_{t_{i+1}}$, the supremum has the closed-form CDF

$$ \Pr\!\left[\sup_{t \in [t_i, t_{i+1}]} X_t \le m\,\Big|\, X_{t_i}, X_{t_{i+1}}\right] = 1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{2(m - X_{t_i})(m - X_{t_{i+1}})}{\sigma^2 (t_{i+1} - t_i)}\right) $$

for $m \ge \max(X_{t_i}, X_{t_{i+1}})$. The MC algorithm therefore: simulate $X_{t_i}$ at all monitoring dates; conditional on each consecutive pair, sample the bridge maximum from the inverse-CDF; declare a touch if any bridge maximum exceeds the log-barrier $\log(B/S_0)$. The result is an *unbiased* estimator of the continuous-monitoring touch probability under Brownian dynamics. The method is presented in canonical form in [Glasserman, *Monte Carlo Methods in Financial Engineering* (Springer 2004), Chapter 6](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-0-387-21617-1.pdf), and the variance reduction is reviewed in [Glasserman and Staum, "Conditioning on one-step survival in barrier option simulations" (Operations Research 49(6), 2001)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/opre.49.6.923.10018).

For non-Brownian models — Heston, Bates, SLV — the Brownian-bridge correction is approximate: between simulation steps the variance is treated as locally constant, the bridge maximum is sampled from the conditional Brownian-bridge formula evaluated at the local volatility, and the bias is one order higher in $\Delta t$ than the discretization scheme of the underlying. This is the standard treatment in [Cont and Tankov (2003)](https://www.routledge.com/Financial-Modelling-with-Jump-Processes/Cont-Tankov/p/book/9781584884132) for jump-diffusion models, where the correction must additionally account for the possibility of an intra-step jump carrying the path over the barrier — handled by simulating jump times from a Poisson process and partitioning the bridge over jump-free sub-intervals.

The companion technique is the **fast Fourier transform** (FFT) of [Carr and Madan, "Option valuation using the fast Fourier transform" (J. Comp. Finance 2(4), 1999)](https://engineering.nyu.edu/sites/default/files/2018-08/CarrMadan2_0.pdf), which prices vanillas under any model with a closed-form characteristic function (Heston, Bates, Kou, VG, CGMY) in milliseconds. FFT alone does not give barrier prices, but it gives the *model's vanilla surface* required for SLV calibration in seconds, after which barriers are priced by MC or PDE on the calibrated SLV. The Carr–Madan technique combined with QE-Andersen MC and Brownian-bridge correction is the workhorse of modern FX-exotics MC engines.

A separate variance-reduction technique relevant for one-touches is the **importance-sampling** approach of [Glasserman, Heidelberger, and Shahabuddin, "Asymptotically optimal importance sampling and stratification for pricing path-dependent options" (Math. Finance 1999)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1467-9965.00067): for a far-out-of-the-money one-touch where the touch probability is small, naive MC gives a noisy estimator because most paths never touch and contribute zero to the sum. Importance-sampling tilts the drift to make the touch likely under the simulation measure, then Radon–Nikodym corrects. Variance reductions of 100x are routine for far-OTM touches, which is the regime that matters for the Phase 14 contract.

## 10. Static replication: Carr–Bowie–Ellis and Derman–Ergener–Kani

A complementary line of attack to dynamic delta-hedging is **static replication**: building a portfolio of vanilla options at trade inception that, with no further rebalancing, replicates the barrier or one-touch payoff on a wide range of paths. Two foundational papers anchor the field. [Derman, Ergener, and Kani, "Static Options Replication" (Journal of Derivatives 2(4), May 1995)](https://jod.iijournals.com/content/2/4/78) (DEK) construct a static replicating portfolio for a knock-out barrier under flat-vol GBM by matching the barrier-option price along the barrier line at a finite set of times. Specifically, for an up-and-out call with strike $K < B$, DEK select strikes equal to the barrier $B$ (with varying maturities) so that the value of the replicating portfolio matches the value of the target payoff (which is zero on the barrier prior to expiry). The closed-form weights are obtained by solving a triangular linear system. The replication is exact in the limit of continuous matching dates and a continuum of vanillas; with a few discrete maturities and tenors, the replication errors are 1–3% of the option value.

[Carr, Ellis, and Gupta, "Static Hedging of Exotic Options" (Journal of Finance 53(3), June 1998)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/0022-1082.00048), with manuscript at [NYU Engineering](https://engineering.nyu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-03/Carr-static-hedging-of-exotic-options.pdf), recast the same problem using the **put-call symmetry** of the Black–Scholes model. Under symmetric vol (i.e., $\sigma$ constant or symmetric in log-strike) and forward-zero drift, a vanilla European call with strike $K$ and a vanilla European put with strike $F^2/K$ (where $F$ is the forward) have payoffs that match along any line through the forward. The static-replication recipe for an up-and-out call with strike $K$ and barrier $B$ is then: hold the long vanilla call at $K$, sell a put at $K' = B^2/K$ with notional adjusted by the strike-ratio, and (in the rebate-paying case) add a static digital at the barrier. The replication is *exact under put-call symmetry* and, importantly, requires only a small (typically 1–2) number of vanillas — far fewer than DEK's continuum. With a real (asymmetric) smile, the static hedge is approximate; the size of the residual depends on the wing-skew and is the subject of [Carr and Chou, "Hedging Complex Barrier Options" (NYU working paper 2002)](https://www.math.nyu.edu/~carrp/papers/pdf/multipl3.pdf) and [Carr and Lee, "Put-call symmetry: extensions and applications" (Math. Finance 2009)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9965.2009.00379.x).

For one-touches specifically, the Carr–Ellis–Gupta static-replication recipe gives an upper-bound hedge for the upside one-touch as a strip of digital calls struck at the barrier with equally-spaced expiries between today and $T$, with the digital cash-or-nothing prices read off the smile via Carr–Madan or Breeden–Litzenberger. The strip becomes exact in the limit of continuously-spaced expiries and is a useful benchmark for sanity-checking a dynamic-hedging-based fair value. The practitioner reference is [Wystup, *FX Options and Structured Products* (2e, Wiley 2017)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/book/10.1002/9781118673515), Chapter 3, which gives the recipe for FX one-touches with the appropriate domestic/foreign rate adjustment.

The strategic value of static replication for an FX market maker is risk-management rather than valuation. Under any reasonable smile, the *price* of the static hedge is close to the *price* of the option, but the *Greeks* of the static hedge are vastly more stable than those of a dynamic delta hedge. Near the barrier and near expiry, the dynamic delta of a one-touch can flip sign daily and produce gamma explosions (Section 11); the static hedge has stable, well-behaved Greeks throughout the life of the trade. The cost is that the static hedge must be entered all at once and unwound only at expiry, which ties up vanilla bid-offer at trade inception and at maturity; the tradeoff is favorable when the underlying is liquid and the contract is large enough that gamma-explosion losses dominate vanilla bid-offer.

## 11. Greeks of one-touches

The Greeks of a continuous-monitoring one-touch under GBM are derived by differentiating the closed form of Section 2; in any non-GBM model they are obtained by bumping the relevant input and recomputing via PDE or MC (with pathwise or likelihood-ratio differentiation as in [Reiss and Wystup, "Efficient Computation of Option Price Sensitivities Using Homogeneity and Other Tricks" (J. Derivatives 9(2), 2001)](https://archive.wias-berlin.de/receive/wias_mods_00001006;lang=en)). The qualitative behavior is the central practical content of this section.

**Delta.** Far from the barrier, the one-touch delta is small and positive (for an upside touch): increasing spot raises the touch probability proportionally to the marginal density of the running maximum at the barrier. Near the barrier and near expiry, the delta accelerates rapidly and approaches a delta close to the funding factor of the rebate at the moment of touch — at which point the contract pays out and the delta jumps to zero. The result is a **delta discontinuity at the touch** (or, in the pay-at-expiry convention, a discontinuity at expiry). Practically, this means a dynamic delta hedge cannot be perfectly executed near the barrier: between the moment a fixing crosses $B$ and the moment the trader can rebalance, the hedge is exposed to a step.

**Gamma.** The gamma of a one-touch is positive far from the barrier (the touch probability is convex in spot for paths well below $B$) and exhibits the classic **gamma explosion** as spot approaches the barrier near expiry. Specifically, $\Gamma \to \infty$ as $S_t \to B$ and $t \to T$ with a rate of $1/[(B - S_t)\sqrt{T-t}]$ in the Black–Scholes one-touch closed form. The economic content is that near a barrier near expiry, the optionality is concentrated entirely in the next few minutes of price action, and the dealer must rebalance at very high frequency to track the delta. Wystup's textbook treatment ([Wystup 2017](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/book/10.1002/9781118673515)) gives the explicit limiting form and the practitioner's mitigation: cap the dynamic-hedge gamma by overlaying a static digital at the barrier.

**Vega.** The vega of a one-touch is the sign-flip Greek that distinguishes touch products from vanillas. For an upside one-touch with $S_0 < B$ and the spot far below the barrier, vega is positive: more vol means more probability of reaching $B$. As spot rises toward the barrier (still untouched), vega remains positive but its magnitude grows. Once the contract is *deep in the money* — meaning the touch is highly likely but has not yet occurred — vega becomes **negative**: more vol now means more variance of paths *that have not yet touched*, which has a negative effect on the conditional touch probability over the remaining life. The sign-flip region depends on the moneyness and time-to-expiry; in the standard Black–Scholes parameterization, vega flips sign at approximately

$$ \log(S_t / B) \approx -\frac{1}{2}\sigma\sqrt{T - t}, $$

i.e., when the spot is roughly half a standard-deviation away from the barrier on the no-touch side. The full derivation of the vega expression and the sign-flip region is in [Hakala and Wystup (2002)](https://www.amazon.com/Foreign-Exchange-Risk-Instruments-Strategies/dp/1899332863) and in the Wystup *FX Options* textbook. Operationally, the vega sign-flip means that a dealer who delta-hedges a long one-touch *and is short vol* at the barrier must rebalance her vol-hedge as the contract goes deep ITM — a non-trivial complication that pure-vanilla dealers rarely encounter.

**Theta.** The theta of an unobserved (yet-untouched) one-touch is *positive* in the convention where time-decay is measured as $-\partial V/\partial t$ counted from the option holder's perspective: probability of touch decays with time remaining, so the option value erodes. For an already-touched (rebate-paying-at-expiry) one-touch, theta is the trivial discount factor accretion, $rV$. The asymmetry between the unobserved-theta and the observed-theta is what makes the one-touch a structurally short-vol product in calm markets: the holder is paying time decay against the chance of a barrier excursion.

**Rho.** The rho of an FX one-touch is two-dimensional (domestic and foreign rate), and its sign depends on whether the rebate is paid in the domestic or the foreign currency. The Reiss–Wystup symmetry tricks ([Reiss and Wystup, *J. Derivatives* 2001](https://archive.wias-berlin.de/receive/wias_mods_00001006;lang=en)) reduce the four FX rate-Greek expressions to two, and the [Wystup, "FX Column: Vanna-Volga and the Greeks"](https://www.mathfinance.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/FX_Column_2020-07-VannaVolgaGreeks.pdf) practitioner column gives the specific FX-one-touch rho expressions used in dealer systems.

The practitioner consequence of the Greek behavior, taken together, is that *long-dated upside one-touches with the barrier well above spot* sit in the well-behaved region of the Greek surface: vega is monotonically positive, gamma is bounded, theta is small and slowly decaying. *Short-dated near-barrier* one-touches sit in the gamma-explosion / vega-sign-flip region and are several orders of magnitude harder to risk-manage. The Phase 14 contract is in the well-behaved regime.

## 12. Smile-dependence of one-touch prices

The most important practical message of this phase is that one-touch prices are *acutely sensitive to the implied volatility smile*, and this sensitivity is concentrated in the wing closer to the barrier. For an upside one-touch on USD/BRL with barrier well above spot at 7-month expiry, the relevant smile parameter is the upside wing-skew measured by the **25-delta risk-reversal**

$$ \text{RR}_{25\Delta} = \sigma_{25\Delta\,\text{call}} - \sigma_{25\Delta\,\text{put}}. $$

The decomposition under the FX-market vanna-volga or the Carr–Lee parametrization shows that, to first order, an upside one-touch price is the GBM price plus a *positive* contribution proportional to upside-wing skew (because more upside-wing density translates into more probability mass on paths that reach $B$) plus a *positive* contribution proportional to vol-of-vol. The most accessible quantitative treatment is in [Hakala and Wystup (2002)](https://www.amazon.com/Foreign-Exchange-Risk-Instruments-Strategies/dp/1899332863), Chapter 7, which gives a closed-form vanna-volga adjustment to the GBM one-touch price as a function of $(\text{ATM}, \text{RR}_{25}, \text{BF}_{25})$.

The Wystup vanna-volga trader-tool calibration ([Castagna and Mercurio, "The Vanna-Volga Method for Implied Volatilities" (Risk Jan. 2007)](https://www.risk.net/derivatives/2189213/vanna-volga-method-implied-volatilities); [Bossens, Rayée, Skantzos, Deelstra, "Vanna-Volga methods applied to FX derivatives" (IJTAF, 2010)](https://www.worldscientific.com/doi/abs/10.1142/S0219024910006030)) gives the standard sell-side recipe for adjusting GBM exotic prices for the smile in the no-arbitrage-with-vanillas sense. The vanna-volga adjustment for an upside FX one-touch sums three components:

$$ V_{\text{VV}} = V_{\text{BS}} + p_{\text{surv}} \cdot \left(w_{\text{vega}} \cdot \Delta\sigma_{\text{ATM}} + w_{\text{vanna}} \cdot \Delta\text{RR}_{25} + w_{\text{volga}} \cdot \Delta\text{BF}_{25}\right), $$

with $p_{\text{surv}}$ a survival probability (the no-touch probability discounted), $\Delta\sigma_{\text{ATM}}$ the difference between ATM market vol and the constant vol used in the BS price, and the weights $w_{\text{vega}}, w_{\text{vanna}}, w_{\text{volga}}$ readable in closed form from the BS model.

For USD/BRL specifically, the structural feature is that the upside RR is large and positive in stress (the market prices BRL depreciation skew much wider than appreciation skew, especially around fiscal-policy or election events), and a fixed-percentage shift in $\text{RR}_{25}$ moves an upside one-touch price by 5–15% multiplicatively. The KTH master's thesis [Pricing FX Barriers with Local Volatility Surface (KTH 2010)](https://www.math.kth.se/matstat/seminarier/reports/M-exjobb10/100510.pdf) reproduces the smile-sensitivity analysis on USD/EM crosses and confirms that the smile is by far the dominant model risk for long-dated upside touches.

The literature on FX-specific smile dependence of barriers is reviewed in [Lipton, *Mathematical Methods for Foreign Exchange* (World Scientific 2001)](https://www.worldscientific.com/worldscibooks/10.1142/4517) and in [Castagna, *FX Options and Smile Risk* (Wiley 2010)](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/FX+Options+and+Smile+Risk-p-9780470754191). The unanimous practitioner conclusion is the one stated at the top: a long-dated FX one-touch price computed without smile information is a number whose error is dominated by the unmodeled smile, and the size of the error can easily be a multiplicative factor of 1.5x–3x.

## 13. Why GBM is a particularly bad model for these contracts

Stitching the previous sections together, the case against pricing a long-dated FX one-touch with flat-vol GBM is overdetermined. Five distinct biases each push the GBM price away from the true price, and several of them push *in the same direction* (under-pricing the touch when the barrier is in the high-skew wing), so the cumulative error compounds rather than partially cancels.

First, the **smile-dependence** of touch products (Section 12). A flat vol cannot represent the upside skew that prices the actual paths reaching the barrier. The vanna-volga or SLV-corrected price is uniformly higher than the flat-vol GBM price for an upside touch on a positively-skewed pair like USD/BRL, by a factor of 1.2x–2x.

Second, the **stochastic-vol effect** (Section 5). Volatility is not constant; vol-spot correlation is empirically negative for equity indices and *positive* for many EM-FX pairs (BRL, MXN, ZAR), meaning that as spot rallies toward the upside barrier, vol *increases*, which raises the conditional touch probability over the remainder of the life. Heston with positive $\rho$ produces upside-touch prices materially higher than GBM with the average ATM vol.

Third, the **jump effect** (Section 8). Even small jump intensities on the order of 0.2–0.5/year materially raise the probability of reaching far-out-of-the-money barriers in long-dated contracts. The Bates SVJ price for an upside touch is typically 1.3x–2x the GBM price for the regime relevant to the Phase 14 contract.

Fourth, the **discrete-monitoring effect** (Section 4) is a *small* downward correction (the daily-fixed barrier touches less often than the continuously-fixed barrier), but it is a known correction and the GBM continuous closed form ignores it.

Fifth, the **dynamics-vs-marginals problem** (Section 6 and 7). Even a Dupire local-vol model that fits every vanilla on the surface produces *wrong* dynamics for forward-starting and barrier products. SLV is required for correct dynamics, and SLV's leverage function has no closed form for one-touches.

These biases compound as follows. Take a hypothetical long-dated upside USD/BRL one-touch with 7 months to expiry, barrier at 1.30x spot, ATM vol 13%, $\text{RR}_{25} = +1.5\%$. Under flat-vol GBM the touch probability is ~3.5%. Under a calibrated SLV with Bates-style upside jumps and ATM-co-moving vol, the touch probability is ~8–12%. The dealer who quotes the GBM price as if it were a fair value is short the touch by a factor of 2–3x — large enough that a single trade can wipe out a year of spread capture.

The practical takeaway, foreshadowing Phase 14: any defensible fair value for the long-dated upside USD/BRL one-touch must, at minimum, (i) reproduce the full vanilla smile (so an SLV calibration to the surface), (ii) reflect FX-specific spot/vol correlation and vol-of-vol (Heston / Bates components inside SLV), (iii) include an explicit jump intensity calibrated either to the wing-skew or to historical jump frequency, (iv) handle discrete monitoring via the BGK shift or its jump-adjusted analogue, and (v) be priced by MC with Brownian-bridge or by 2D PDE with absorbing-barrier boundary. Anything less is a known-biased number, and the bias is not negligible for the regime that matters.
